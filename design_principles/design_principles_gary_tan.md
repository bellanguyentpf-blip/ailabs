# Design Principles

## Messaging & Clarity

### Don't Be Clever If Clever Equals Confusing

Your tagline should be immediately understandable to someone who doesn't know your product. Wordplay that requires insider knowledge fails.

> "sometimes I think we see this from Founders where they want to be cute with their tagline and sometimes the meaning gets lost right but they think it's clever because they understand what they do right but it makes it hard for new people to quickly understand you don't want to be clever if clever equals confusing"

**Bad Example:** [Rosebud](#case-study-rosebud) — "I couldn't tell if game development is for game development or a play on words for like two game like development in general"

---

### Answer "What Is This?" and "Is It For Me?" Immediately

Every visitor needs to instantly understand what your product does and whether it's relevant to them.

> "that's like the first thing that people are trying to understand when they hit a website is what is this and number two being is it for me"

**Bad Example:** [Reality Defender](#case-study-reality-defender) — "we're halfway through the page and we still don't really know what they're doing yet"

**Good Example:** [Voiceflow](#case-study-voiceflow) — "the interaction image was so good I sort of already got what it was for and whether or not it was for me that's how you know that that uh it's well designed"

---

### Write For Your Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)

Your homepage should speak directly to your target customer, not try to appeal to everyone.

> "you want um your ideal customer or your ICP to sort of look at it and say oh that's for me yeah and in order to have that you actually have to write the home page for that particular customer not like a nebulous like any customer anyone who might need our technology like being too General is actually a little bit problematic here"

> "being specific is way better"

**Bad Example:** [Reality Defender](#case-study-reality-defender) — Unclear whether it's for social media platforms, enterprises, or government agencies

---

### Assume Users Won't Try Hard

Most visitors will give up quickly if they can't understand your product. Don't make them work for it.

> "we're halfway through the page and we're still really looking and we still don't quite understand what it is and we're trying and I think most users when they hit this they're not actually going to try this hard"

**Bad Example:** [Reality Defender](#case-study-reality-defender) — Required extensive scrolling and reading to understand the product

---

### Use Your Customers' Words

Talk to your users and use the exact language they use to describe your product.

> "one of the best tactics to be able to do this go talk to your existing users ask them how would you describe my product and then take the exact phrase that they say and just put that up at the top of your site because that's ideally the same thing that will resonate for other potential customers"

---

### Make Your Differentiation Obvious

Visitors comparing similar products need to understand what makes you different.

> "we've seen a lot of these reduce Cloud spend companies and so I'm kind of wondering with my YC partner hat on like what's different about what these guys are doing and probably that's something that potential customers will be thinking too if you search for like cut Cloud costs probably dozens of potential products that you could go with to help with that so like why these guys is probably the question a lot of their users are going to be asking"

**Bad Example:** [Pump](#case-study-pump) — "it's not clear to me what's different like I guess there's AI but like lots of others are probably going to have ai too so I'm not convinced that's enough of a differentiation"

---

### Be Explicit About Your Core Mechanism

If you have a unique approach, don't bury it—hit people over the head with it.

> "there's this Groupon thing which makes me think there's some like group buying aspect where maybe it's like multiple companies pooling together in order to get on a better tier with lower pricing would be my hunch but they don't actually really say that anywhere"

> "it feels like one of those things that you should be people over the head with to make it super obvious that that's what's happening here and that's why it's better"

**Bad Example:** [Pump](#case-study-pump) — The collective/group buying mechanism was buried instead of being the lead message

---

### Focus On One Thing

Don't try to showcase multiple products or use cases on your homepage. Pick your primary offering.

> "it seems like there's at least four different products here which is kind of confusing because usually you want a single call to action that you're driving people towards"

> "we have to be a little bit more opinionated about what we really want the user to click on"

**Bad Example:** [Rosebud](#case-study-rosebud) — Featured game assets, sprites, sky boxes, and talking heads all on one page

---

## Visual Design & Polish

### Spacing Signals Quality and Trust

Proper spacing and visual hierarchy convey professionalism, especially for enterprise products.

> "having really good spacing it just sort of contributes to this feeling of quality and when you have little fit and finish issues like you know certain things being too close or headers having the wrong line height it does ruin the Illusion for me that this is something that I can trust at the Enterprise level"

> "there's really too much spacing between them between the lines of this header and then there's not enough spacing between the logos and this bottom body area"

**Bad Example:** [Reality Defender](#case-study-reality-defender) — Spacing issues undermined the enterprise positioning

---

### Small Quality Issues Compound

Even if most users won't consciously notice fit-and-finish problems, they still erode overall quality perception.

> "does it really matter you know majority of people probably won't tell but for some people it will matter and it just brings down the quality by that like little five or ten percent"

**Bad Example:** [Reality Defender](#case-study-reality-defender) — Minor spacing and typography issues accumulated to hurt credibility

---

### Use Animation Selectively to Direct Attention

Animation should guide users to what matters, not create sensory overload.

> "a lot of times animation can be used effectively to drop the user's eye to something and if a lot of things are moving then it just becomes kind of overwhelming and I think being selective about it so it's what's the thing that you really want to draw people's attention to what's the next step you want them to take and then put animation around that"

**Bad Example:** [Pump](#case-study-pump) — "a lot of sensory overload yeah like a lot of colors a lot of movement a lot of graphs like it's cool but I'm not sure that I watched that and I'm like oh yeah I know what these guys do"

**Good Example:** [Voiceflow](#case-study-voiceflow) — "there was animation there's motion but it actually made us intrigued it wasn't like oh man I need to put on my sunglasses"

---

### Less Is More

Restrained design builds more trust than flashy visuals.

> "less is more here less is more"

> "reduce down some of the motion and animation and bright colors and instead use it sparingly to draw attention to specific things"

**Good Example:** [Voiceflow](#case-study-voiceflow) — "I actually like how it chilled out this scroll is this is a good contrast to the last one I was like boom like so much Movement Like You know I'm you know I'm getting dizzy and here it's like oh we're chill"

---

### Use Contrast to Guide Users

Visual weight should clearly indicate the primary action you want users to take.

> "one of the most important things to use is contrast in order to figure out in order to be opinionated to the user I want you to do this"

> "if you really want people to try the beta make that the call to action and then make learn more instead of a button that's equal weight just make it a link"

**Bad Example:** [Rosebud](#case-study-rosebud) — Two equal-weight buttons confused users about which action to take

## Calls to Action

### Have One Primary CTA

Don't give users multiple equal options. Be opinionated about what you want them to do.

> "figure out what their main kpi is and what outcome they want from people that hit their website my hunch is it's not go explore all of these products or figure out whichever one is most interesting to you and try that there's probably one that's their main priority here"

**Bad Example:** [Rosebud](#case-study-rosebud) — "two obvious call to actions one is a gaming asset generator and then the other one is AI Game Dev platform... I'm confused I don't know which to click"

---

### Make CTAs Action-Oriented and Specific

The best CTAs promise immediate, tangible value.

> "view my savings makes it feel just very present to hand like oh I could do it very quickly"

**Good Example:** [Pump](#case-study-pump) — "View My Savings" as a CTA was effective because it promised immediate, personal value

---

### Minimize Friction to the Aha Moment

Every click is an opportunity for users to drop off. Get them to value as fast as possible.

> "every time you move people uh make them do extra clicks you have risk of drop off some percentage are not going to do it they're going to miss out on this"

> "get them directly into that product to get the aha moment as quickly as possible"

**Needs Improvement:** [Rosebud](#case-study-rosebud) — "how could you just have this right on the home page so people don't even have to click"

---

### Don't Put Walls Before Value

Let users experience your product before requiring sign-up.

> "do your first one free like don't even actually force people to create an account and put that wall up front instead you could just make it accessible and if somebody wants to save it or use it or however they plan to use it that's when you prompt them to actually create the account because they're already invested in the product"

> "you're always fighting the urge for the back button or to go look at Hacker News"

**Bad Example:** [Magic Flow](#case-study-magic-flow) — "when you click through and it's like try it now and you hit this wall there's an even context about like why you should sign up here"

---

### Don't Break Trust with False Interactivity

If you promise interaction ("try it," "drag and drop"), it must actually work.

> "I inherently tried to drag and drop because it felt like I should be able to do that they said that multiple times drag and drop they said drag and drop so the first thing I tried to do is and then when I moused over this thing it actually becomes a hand cursor which is also the affordance for right being able to drag so what happens when you click on it absolutely nothing"

> "the problem with this interaction is it just doesn't doesn't build trust with me"

**Bad Example:** [Magic Flow](#case-study-magic-flow) — "okay now you're just lying to me"

---

## Demonstrating Value

### Show a Feat of Strength

For AI products, demonstrate what's newly possible. Show the magic.

> "is there a way that you can make a video or use animation that actually shows the product and then shows in particular for AI a feat of strength so it's like check this out you've never been able to do this before you know type a few things and then look at this magic output I mean those are the types of demos that really well people and that's actually why people are so obsessed with this technology now like the demos and the technological Feats of Strength that people are capable of putting out like that's just blowing people away"

**Good Example:** [Voiceflow](#case-study-voiceflow) — The animated demo immediately conveyed the product's capabilities

---

### Use Concrete Examples, Not Abstract Workflows

Nobody searches for "workflow software." Show specific use cases that match real problems.

> "nobody's like man I really need workflow software instead there's a specific problem that they're looking to solve and so usually what works kind of to your point earlier is to actually focus on very specific use cases that are the most common ones and use that to get people into the product and then they can say oh there's actually much more that I can do here too"

**Needs Improvement:** [Magic Flow](#case-study-magic-flow) — Abstract workflow demonstration instead of concrete use case

---

### Include a "How It Works" Section Early

Help users build mental models quickly.

> "how it works I like I really like when they do how it works very early because it brings you to a much higher understanding of what's going on it's kind of like when you do YC interviews and you get to do a demo"

**Good Example:** [Magic Flow](#case-study-magic-flow) — "How it works" section was well-positioned

---

### Show Real Integrations and Technologies

Displaying familiar tools helps users understand if your product fits their workflow.

> "I like that they're using hugging face and stability Ai and chat I mean these are you know being able to pull models from replicate great like most everyone's sort of replicate at this point and they know okay this is where I get a particular open source model to use and I think a lot of people will see those and it'll resonate like oh I'm using chat GPT or I'm using hugging face or whatever and and that makes it feel like oh it's for me"

**Good Example:** [Magic Flow](#case-study-magic-flow) — Showing integrations with ChatGPT, Hugging Face, Stability AI, Replicate

---

### Address Common Objections Proactively

If your users have typical concerns, address them directly on the page.

> "what I like about that section is it feels like it came from lots of conversations with users like the faster cheaper kind of thing is probably the thing that when they talk to users they would tell them were their concerns about using a product like theirs or why don't I just build it myself or something like that um so it seems like they've proactively addressed that which is great"

**Good Example:** [Magic Flow](#case-study-magic-flow) — Addressed "30 percent cheaper and 25 faster" concerns

---

## Social Proof & Trust

### Use Case Studies with Real Results

Generic logos aren't enough. Show specific customer wins.

> "what would help me is if there was a customer that I had heard of that had used this already and saved X percent"

> "I almost want there to be a case study with like whoever the number one customer is"

**Needs Improvement:** [Pump](#case-study-pump) — Had logos but no case studies with specific results

---

### Weave Social Proof Throughout the Page

Don't just dump logos in one section. Connect testimonials to relevant features.

> "it's nice how they keep um weaving the different logos and customers throughout as they're making a different point and then they have somebody from that company that's giving a quote that reinforces that it's actually real people at this company it's not like one user tried it one time and then you know stopped using it afterwards yeah this makes it uh very very trustworthy"

**Good Example:** [Voiceflow](#case-study-voiceflow) — Logos and quotes strategically placed throughout

---

### Use Real People and Names

Faces and names make testimonials feel authentic.

> "I like that this is a a coherent point and they've got Molly's face or Colin's face there and uh it feels like real people rather than something that's made up"

**Good Example:** [Voiceflow](#case-study-voiceflow) — Named individuals with photos gave credibility

---

### Use FAQs to Complete the Sale

Well-positioned FAQs can answer the questions preventing conversion.

> "having an FAQ present to hand means that you can sort of pique someone's interest and then get their you know this is actually a good experience then like if within two clicks you can get you know and actually having these two be the first two that's a good choice"

**Good Example:** [Pump](#case-study-pump) — FAQ section answered "How does the group buying work?"

---

### Learn from Established Products

Study products that have been around longer—they've validated their design through iteration.

> "it seems nicely designed it seems like lots of people have used it and given feedback on it and they've iterated to this point which is actually another good way to learn is to look at other products that have been around for a while and take some of the techniques that they use and it's really easy to like they've already validated it they've spent years refining it like you get to take their learnings for free right out of the gate"

**Good Example:** [Voiceflow](#case-study-voiceflow) — Showed signs of refined, iterated design

# Case Studies

## Case Study: Rosebud

**Website:** AI game development platform  
**Product:** AI-generated game assets and game development tools

### What Worked
- Had a beta to try: "try beta means oh this isn't vaporware"
- Multiple media/social proof elements present
- Input field for game ideas was a good start

### What Failed
- **Confusing tagline:** "AI generated game assets to game development build games at the speed of thought GPT to game that's a lot of that's three different concepts uh all at the same time"
- **Wordplay backfires:** "I couldn't tell if game development is for game development or a play on words for like two game like development in general"
- **Too many products:** "it seems like there's at least four different products here which is kind of confusing"
- **Competing CTAs:** "two obvious call to actions one is a gaming asset generator and then the other one is AI Game Dev platform... I'm confused I don't know which to click"
- **Distracting hero animation:** "there's a busy thing on the left that's sort of distracting me"
- **Extra clicks to value:** "every time you move people uh make them do extra clicks you have risk of drop off"
- **Missing suggestions:** "what would be helpful is like even a couple things to click on like even a fly out here that's you know just give me some good ideas just impress me"

### Key Lessons
- Pick one primary product to feature
- Use one clear CTA, make secondary actions into links
- Make taglines literal rather than clever
- Put example prompts or one-click demos on the homepage
- Show the AI output/feat of strength immediately
- Move secondary products to their own pages

## Case Study: Magic Flow

**Website:** AI workflow automation  
**Product:** Drag-and-drop tool to create AI workflows

### What Worked
- **Early "How it Works" section:** "I really like when they do how it works very early because it brings you to a much higher understanding"
- **Integration logos build familiarity:** "I like that they're using hugging face and stability Ai and chat... I think a lot of people will see those and it'll resonate"
- **Proactive objection handling:** "it feels like it came from lots of conversations with users like the faster cheaper kind of thing"
- **Specific claims:** "it is good to use very concrete claims like it's 30 percent cheaper and 25 faster"
- **Product demo animation:** "this is actually pretty good it's sort of showing hey this is what we can do"

### What Failed
- **Premature CTA:** "I feel like the flow is not that people come in they read this first paragraph and they immediately click the button"
- **Sign-up wall before value:** "when you click through and it's like try it now and you hit this wall there's an even context about like why you should sign up here"
- **False interactivity:** "it actually becomes a hand cursor which is also the affordance for right being able to drag so what happens when you click on it absolutely nothing"
- **Broken trust:** "the problem with this interaction is it just doesn't doesn't build trust with me... okay now you're just lying to me"
- **Important content buried:** "there's great stuff here it's just kind of small and like buried on the side there"
- **Abstract workflow example:** Could be clearer about specific use cases rather than generic workflows

### Key Lessons
- Let users try the product without signing up first
- If you say "drag and drop" or "try," it must actually work
- Make important value props bigger and more prominent
- Show specific use cases, not abstract workflows
- Create an account wall only after users are invested

## Case Study: Reality Defender

**Website:** Deep fake detection platform  
**Product:** AI-powered detection of fabricated media

### What Worked
- **Strong social proof:** "government approved and go looks like government agencies are using this in visa and Microsoft so like they sell me on on that piece which intrigues me to want to learn more like I take it seriously"
- **Case study present:** "how a national public broadcaster detects deep fakes got it I mean that sounds pretty important"
- **Serious logos build credibility:** "NATO uses it"

### What Failed
- **Misleading hero video:** "this makes me think this is like software for like closed circuit cameras right like to find people walking through the airport... but it talks about deep fakes so it seems like maybe that's not actually what's happening here"
- **Still unclear halfway through:** "we're halfway through the page and we're still really looking and we still don't quite understand what it is and we're trying"
- **Users won't work this hard:** "I think most users when they hit this they're not actually going to try this hard"
- **No clear target customer:** "I'm still confused... that's one thing that we're always looking for in the home page like I you want um your ideal customer or your ICP to sort of look at it and say oh that's for me"
- **Spacing issues hurt credibility:** "having really good spacing it just sort of contributes to this feeling of quality and when you have little fit and finish issues... it does ruin the Illusion for me that this is something that I can trust at the Enterprise level"
- **Small issues compound:** "does it really matter you know majority of people probably won't tell but for some people it will matter and it just brings down the quality by that like little five or ten percent"
- **Maslow's hierarchy of messaging:** "the most important thing who is it who's it for you got to know that it's like Maslow's hierarchy you can't progress to The Next Step"
- **Missing demonstration:** "I kind of want to be impressed especially when you're talking about AI there's some sort of feet of strength that you can show"

### Key Lessons
- Explain who your customer is and what problem you solve above the fold
- Video should directly demonstrate the actual product, not adjacent concepts
- Fix spacing and typography for enterprise credibility
- Lead with your clearest use case before expanding to others
- Show the AI doing something impressive
- Case studies should be more prominent with specific data

## Case Study: Pump

**Website:** AWS cost optimization  
**Product:** Group buying collective for cloud infrastructure savings

### What Worked
- **Action-oriented CTA:** "view my savings makes it feel just very present to hand like oh I could do it very quickly"
- **Strong social proof with logos:** "I like the social proof that makes sense gotta do that"
- **FAQ addresses key questions:** "having an FAQ present to hand means that you can sort of pique someone's interest"
- **Good experience flow:** "if within two clicks you can get you know and actually having these two be the first two that's a good choice"
- **Engaging video music:** "I like the tunes"

### What Failed
- **Sensory overload:** "a lot of sensory overload yeah like a lot of colors a lot of movement a lot of graphs like it's cool but I'm not sure that I watched that and I'm like oh yeah I know what these guys do"
- **Video doesn't explain:** "I don't know if I need to watch a Super Bowl ad first yeah like that's not the thing that's going to get me to use it"
- **Buried differentiation:** "they keep throwing out this Groupon thing which makes me think there's some like group buying aspect... but they don't actually really say that anywhere"
- **Missing case study with results:** "what would help me is if there was a customer that I had heard of that had used this already and saved X percent"
- **Unclear competitive advantage:** "it's not clear to me what's different like I guess there's AI but like lots of others are probably going to have ai too"
- **Animation overuse:** "a lot of the bright colors and moving elements um maybe potentially overused"

### Key Lessons
- Make your unique mechanism (group buying) the lead message
- Use animation selectively to direct attention, not everywhere
- Add case studies with specific savings numbers
- Clearly differentiate from competitors
- Less is more with visual effects
- Video should explain, not just entertain

## Case Study: Voiceflow

**Website:** Voice and chat assistant builder  
**Product:** Collaborative platform for designing, testing, and launching AI assistants

**Overall Assessment:** "well done voice flow yeah this is great"

### What Worked
- **Immediately understandable:** "I can visually understand that here's a flow chart it's literally called voice flow"
- **Clear product demonstration:** "the interaction image was so good I sort of already got what it was for and whether or not it was for me that's how you know that that uh it's well designed"
- **Restrained animation:** "I actually like how it chilled out this scroll is this is a good contrast to the last one... here it's like oh we're chill like it's yeah don't worry about it"
- **Motion that intrigues:** "there was animation there's motion but it actually made us intrigued it wasn't like oh man I need to put on my sunglasses"
- **Strong social proof:** "a hundred thousand teams that's nice... if all of these big companies are actually using their product well that's why they can be chill about it"
- **Woven testimonials:** "it's nice how they keep um weaving the different logos and customers throughout as they're making a different point and then they have somebody from that company that's giving a quote"
- **Real people, real trust:** "they've got Molly's face or Colin's face there and uh it feels like real people rather than something that's made up"
- **Design credibility:** "loved by designers great I mean makes sense we can tell yeah game recognized game"
- **Clear hierarchy and CTAs:** "clear call to actions nicely designed good hierarchy and flow I think we understand what it is the right things drew our attention"
- **Shows iteration and maturity:** "it seems nicely designed it seems like lots of people have used it and given feedback on it and they've iterated to this point"
- **Collaboration feature highlighted:** "they're making a big deal of multiple people being on the same web page sort of collaboratively be able to build it which is that is a cool feature"

### What Failed
- **Animation speed:** "it's moving a little too fast for me to dig in and be able to tell what is actually being connected like what this flow is"
- **Could use concrete example:** "to give a concrete example of who would use this and why I think would be helpful"

### Key Lessons
- Name your product literally (Voiceflow = voice + flowchart)
- Use restrained, purposeful animation
- Weave social proof throughout the page, not just in one section
- Include real names and faces with testimonials
- Show collaboration features if that's a differentiator
- Study and learn from mature products' design patterns
- Confidence in design lets you be "chill"
