# Design Principles

## Brand Authenticity & Stage Awareness

### Match Your Website Polish to Your Product Maturity

Don't pretend to be a mature company when you're an early-stage startup. Over-polishing creates mismatched expectations.

> "These days there's maybe this trend of and like because it's easy to build websites you might try to like build very polished or fancy websites very early on. But what that kind of like tells you is like it's maybe sets the wrong expectation for the user that if your product is just that I don't you build it last month and like you don't have everything figured out yet the product might not be there yet. It's not that polished."

> "So I think like there there's the danger that you go too far like trying to look like a large company or like a mature company too early."

**Good Example:** [Linear (Early)](#case-study-linear-evolution-example) — "It's it's something like we created in basically one day we didn't want to spend weeks or months trying to create a website where we think like the actual the effort should be put put into the product itself."

---

### Don't Copy Successful Companies' Current State

Every successful brand started simple. Their current website represents years of evolution, not their starting point.

> "I think these days maybe a lot of startups look at I don't know stripe or some lower linear or some some other company out there and they they think like okay this is the the way to build sites or this is the way to build your brand none of those companies started that way like stripe were first website was very different than the current website um as as well as their product and similar with linear like the first website was very different than the the current website."

**Good Example:** [Linear](#case-study-linear-evolution-example) — "This maybe like a third evolution of the of the site and um like the second one was the kind of famous purple website that was like like replicated a lot uh across the companies."

---

### Own Being Small

Embrace your startup identity rather than hiding it. There are advantages to being small.

> "That's such an interesting point which is it sounds like what you're saying is like own being small, own being a startup and there's a lot of advantages that come with that rather than trying to pretend that you're some big company."

---

### Evolve Your Brand With Your Company

Your website should grow and change as your customer base and product matures.

> "I think it's it's something that you should think that the the website that the brand always evolves with your company and your customer base. So even now like we have more larger companies enterprises. I don't want to turn into like a fully like enterprise oracle like company like a brand or something but I do still have to think about it's like is this something that fits to the the current customers."

## Messaging & Copy

### Be Specific About What You Do

Use precise, concrete language that your target users actually search for and understand.

> "We wanted to use this issue tracking term because like if if you go to any large company website like you go to Slack or you go some other productivity app website it often says something like this is the work platform or this is the where the work happens or something something very vague right and I think like that comes later when you're trying to build this narrative but in the very beginning what you're trying to sell is you try to find someone to actually use your product and like just understand what it is."

> "You can talk to investors in very ambitious ways. You can say like yeah this is going to be the work platform of the future but like on the website you can just put like this is a chat app and like this is a nice chat app and like just do that."

**Good Example:** [Linear (Early)](#case-study-linear-evolution-example) — "We knew that that wasn't the in like long-term vision for the product or the company, but we knew that that's like something that the people we are looking for. It's just early stage companies and engineers will understand like this is issue tracking is a is a technical project management in a way. It doesn't attract people that don't know what issue tracking is."

**Bad Example:** [Sprites AI](#case-study-sprites-ai) — "I'm not exactly sure who is this for and what can I do with it... streamline growth is well there's all kinds of growth like revenue growth and user growth there's marketing."

---

### Use Language to Filter Your Audience

Be specific to attract the right users and filter out those who aren't a good fit.

> "It's like you're trying to maybe filter out people. You should be very specific initially."

> "It doesn't attract people that don't know what issue tracking is."

---

### Specify Who It's For

Generic products need clear audience segments to help users self-identify.

> "I I would like probably like go a little more specific on on the on the copy here that there is some kind of templates I guess for different kinds of workflows. It's kind of like a random list of different kinds of workflows. So it would if it would be like meaningful to or like useful to group them somehow that hey there's workflows for I don't know like YouTubers or there's workflows for business people or some kind of analysts or something."

**Bad Example:** [Sprites AI](#case-study-sprites-ai) — "Is it for designers is it for um engineers is it for like YouTubers there's something there's a there's a card about YouTube there."

---

### Differentiate From Competition

Clearly articulate what makes you different, not just what you do.

> "I think you should look at like what are those companies saying how do you actually different from those company like what and then like try to highlight those like benefits or special this you have so like rather than saying like well we do evils but like maybe you can say like we do it differently because of these reasons or something."

> "Sometimes there's not that much competition. Maybe there sometimes there is a lot of competition. So you should like make try to think like what is our lane here? How could we make it like special some way or something differentiated and then try to like weave it into the website or into copy or into the graphics or or something."

**Bad Example:** [Confident AI](#case-study-confident-ai) — "My first question is like well how is this different from something else or something like is there something specific about this that makes it so good."

---

### Use Your First Customers' Words

Let real customer feedback shape your messaging.

> "If you don't know then you can go talk to them. It's like why would you pick this product or why would why why did you pick this product and then see if there's some kind of pattern there. It's like well I like like it because of these reasons and then like we'll put those reasons on the web page so like maybe you can find more of them."

---

### Headlines Must Work When Scanned Alone

Users scroll and read only headlines. Make sure they communicate your value without the body text.

> "A lot of times people will scroll and just kind of like read the headlines and decide if they want to dive in deeper on the smaller text. And if I just read that like build in a weekend, validate in minutes, I'm not exactly sure. Make forward progress always. I don't exactly know what those things mean."

> "I think it's helpful sometimes when building a site to, you know, do an exercise or just go down just read the headlines. And does that communicate everything that you want to communicate to the user?"

**Bad Example:** [Confident AI](#case-study-confident-ai) — "Build in a weekend, validate in minutes... Make forward progress always. I don't exactly know what those things mean."

---

### Differentiate Without Direct Comparison

Don't say "we're better than X" — instead, highlight what makes you unique.

> "I don't think you should like make compare it to yourself to the competition like directly like say like hey we do better this and that like or something like that but like I think you should look at like what are those companies saying how do you actually different from those company like what and then like try to highlight those like benefits or special this you have."

> "Rather than saying like well we do evils but like maybe you can say like we do it differently because of these reasons or something."

---

### Find Your One Thing for Early Adopters

Identify what single attribute will make the right early users choose you.

> "As a small startup, you can't be the best at all the things that all users will care about... just trying to figure out what's the one thing that we want to be better than everybody else at. And anybody who cares about that one thing, they're going to choose us."

> "You should think what are some of the things or the one thing that your first type of like ideal customer and then in the beginning especially like how do you make the website to talk to those people."

**Good Example:** [Linear](#case-study-linear-evolution-example) — "The speed was one of the main problems we heard from a lot of people we talked to that they just don't like the tools because they're slow... who who cares most about speed like startups do? People move fast."

## Visual Design & Motion

### Avoid Distracting Background Movement

Moving backgrounds and constant animations make text hard to read and pull attention away from your message.

> "The movement on the cards which I think can be nice but it might not be the best thing to have this looping movement in the in the landing on in the first screen because maybe what happened to you is like you're kind of kind of focused on the movement and like what's coming up next."

> "The moving graphic is probably not meaningful because it's making hard to read the text."

> "If that moving grid went away, I don't think anything would be lost. In fact, it might be gained."

**Bad Example:** [Sprites AI](#case-study-sprites-ai) — "There's this movement in the background and the movement on the cards."

**Bad Example:** [Dropback](#case-study-dropback) — "There's also like a lot of automatic movement on this page which I I do think like adds to the chaos. So there could be some of that but like I would like like basically every section is almost like moving."

---

### Distinguish Helpful Animation from Distracting Animation

Animation should draw attention to important elements, not pull attention away from your message.

> "There's like good animation which draws your attention to um important things that you want users to pay attention to or notice and then there's distracting animation that maybe takes away attention from whatever you're trying to focus on."

---

### Use Screenshots Strategically

Screenshots should create intrigue, not overwhelm with details.

> "I kind of like use this screenshot to show like hey there is some kind of product even though at this time we didn't really have it ready but if we were working on it. So there is some kind of product. It kind of looks nice. You can still bit see like what the features might be but it's a little bit faded. So it's a little bit mysterious."

> "A lot of times I think people put a screenshot on and they just put the literal screenshot and a lot of times it doesn't work because people are not familiar with the app and you're not trying to communicate all the different features and everything like that through this. You're just trying to kind of build mystery and be like this looks like a modern tool."

**Good Example:** [Linear (Early)](#case-study-linear-evolution-example) — Screenshot was "a little bit faded. So it's a little bit mysterious. So, it's like kind of like curious because in the end what we want people to do is put their email here."

---

### Design for User Context, Not in a Vacuum

Consider existing user behaviors and what UI patterns they're trained to ignore.

> "You're not designing in a vacuum of your page. You're designing in the context of what users are used to. And if users are used to ignoring cookie banners and things like that that tend to pop up at the bottom, then like maybe they're just ignore your thing there, too."

**Bad Example:** [Sprites AI](#case-study-sprites-ai) — "This looks like a cookie banner in a way... you kind of often often ignore that."

---

### Product UI Patterns May Not Work in Marketing

UI elements that work inside a product may be ignored or misinterpreted on a marketing site.

> "Those kind of banners like the the UI itself like this could work in the product itself like when you're when you're looking at the like a page or some kind of document like it it could be nice to have this like a floating thing but in the website context it feels like it's a some kind of banner that I don't have to pay attention to."

**Bad Example:** [Sprites AI](#case-study-sprites-ai) — Floating prompter looked like a dismissible banner.

## Information Architecture

### Keep the Hero Simple, Expand Below

Structure your page like a pyramid — simple at the top, expanding with detail as users scroll.

> "The website should be kind of like this almost like this pyramid of like you start with this is what it is and then it's like it's very like as simple as possible and then you keep going down, it's like expands and like okay well there's this thing and that thing and there's more more and more things."

> "The top should be just again like fairly simple that this is this is what we do."

> "I think it's fine to share it like down the page. You just you don't have to do everything at the top."

**Good Example:** [Linear](#case-study-linear-evolution-example) — "The landing page, the first the homepage is kind of like the the front page. So like you can't explain everything like you can't explain all the features, you can't explain all the customer stories and everything. That's why the there's other pages."

**Bad Example:** [Dropback](#case-study-dropback) — "They feel like they got to stuff everything into this front thing... that users will never scroll."

---

### Trust Users to Scroll

Modern users are comfortable scrolling. Don't cram everything above the fold.

> "I think these days people are pretty used to to scrolling. Like even on your phone like you're constantly scrolling up and down and and and so I don't think it's necessarily a problem."

---

### Use Navigation to Create Targeted Pages

Instead of one long page, create dedicated pages for different audiences and use cases.

> "The other way to do this is like you just create other pages. There's currently a lot of space in the navigation that you could say like for college programs for I don't know coaches or something like you could make very clear like targeted pages for specific people or specific use cases and so because people often might look at the navigation and then they're like oh like I want to see what that is."

**Needs Improvement:** [Dropback](#case-study-dropback) — Navigation just linked further down the page rather than to dedicated sections.

---

### Homepage Is for New Visitors

Design your homepage for people who don't know you yet.

> "I always think like the homepage should be for the people who don't really know about you. So it should give you some kind of enough information that you know that is this interesting or not but not too much so that you get overwhelmed and not too little that you don't understand what it is."

---

### Resist Feature Creep on the Homepage

As your product grows, resist the urge to add everything to your homepage.

> "You've resisted the urge to make it overwhelming people with features and you know obviously the product has grown a lot your customer base has grown a lot the company has grown a lot and I think a lot of times the temptation is to want to throw more and more stuff you know on the page to communicate that to anybody who's coming here and it seems like you've actively fought against that."

---

### Show One Clear Workflow Example

For complex products, blow up one example to show how it actually works.

> "I don't think the page um exactly describes how does it work like it might be worthwhile try trying to add some kind of almost like a flowchart not really but explain that what it like take one example and then like kind of like blow it up a little bit like this is what a workflow could look like hey there's like other workflows we created, but like I guess maybe you can create your own too."

**Bad Example:** [Sprites AI](#case-study-sprites-ai) — "A lot of this um there's this promotion on the templates that you can have different kinds of templates but I don't think the page um exactly describes how does it work."

---

### Use a Social Mission as Your CTA

For mission-driven products, invite users to join the cause, not just the product.

> "They seem to have this like a like a more like a broader social mission or kind of like a just cause that we should to live in a world where we don't have to like use the animals this way which is I think something like you could do more here is that hey join this cause like we could change the world kind of that that and then that's the call to action join the mailing list."

**Needs Improvement:** [Unreal Milk](#case-study-unreal-milk) — Could leverage their sustainability mission as a stronger call to action.

---

### Use Email Signups to Learn About Your Audience

Email lists help you identify patterns about who's interested before you have product-market fit.

> "Just like people who like if people land here like even if that not that many like people sign up, you could start some kind of community. Maybe you have a lot of people like students signing up or you maybe have someone else signing up and then you can start seeing this patterns of well who who are who is signing up and then you can ask them like why did you sign up like why is it interesting and then you can kind of like start the conversation."

## Typography & Color

### Font Choice Is Critical for Websites

Websites are primarily textual. Choose fonts that match your brand's personality.

> "I think like I think a font or a typeface is very very big thing on any website because websites in the end are textual. So there's text on websites."

---

### Match Typography to Your Brand World

Consider what world your brand lives in and what signals you want to send.

> "What is the what what is the world the brand is living in and like what kind of signaling you want to do. Sometimes you want to do the serifs or some of the like more like retro or older kind of feeling to it or sometimes like a lot of more modern kind of geometric type faces. It's more like the the kind of like the standard of the day and that's like considered modern. Then there can be like more futuristic type faces."

**Good Example:** [Unreal Milk](#case-study-unreal-milk) — "Their font choice is good here given the the style of it. It's more like handdrawn kind of environment."

---

### Pair Readable Fonts for Small Sizes

Use unique fonts for headlines but standard fonts for body text.

> "It's not like you should just use one typeface everything because in small sizes a lot of type faces don't work. You you probably have to use something fairly standard so people can read it. But for headlines and special points you can you can pair it with something like more unique."

---

### Yellow Is Hard to Use Well

Yellow lacks contrast and is difficult to use for buttons and text.

> "I think there's not enough contrast. I think yellow is a hard like you should go darker with it or not use yellow because it's a little bit hard color to use for button."

> "I think yellow is good, but it's like maybe not the best background color."

**Bad Example:** [Dropback](#case-study-dropback) — "The yellow color is a little bit again like I think there's not enough contrast... white text, white logos are harder to read."

---

### Ensure Text Is Readable

If users struggle to read your headline, nothing else matters.

> "Maybe this text could be even bigger. I I feel find it it's a little bit hard to read and it's like quite small and I think you're just trying to quickly explain like what it is."

---

### Colors Should Match Website and Product

Your brand presentation should be consistent across touchpoints.

> "The font is very like handdrawn here but then the brand on the bottle or the the logo itself is very normal. It's like I don't know if it's Helvetica or what, but like it's the website is very fun and playful and the bottle is not."

**Needs Improvement:** [Unreal Milk](#case-study-unreal-milk) — Missed opportunity to carry fun brand onto product packaging.

## Trust & Credibility

### Build Trust for Your Specific Audience

Understand what your target audience needs to feel confident.

> "At Coinbase when I was working there. I think that I I found that like that the job I had with the brand or with the with the design itself is like creating trust and because you are dealing with money and cryptocurrency back in 2014 wasn't what it is today and like even today there's still trust concerns. It's like can I trust this company to hold my money."

> "You could have like few things that you really want to do and in I don't know this audience but it could be that they just want to see something trustworthy and you should just signal that like this is trustworthy we have to kind of like the features you need."

---

### Choose Feature-Based or Use Case-Based Structure

Match your page structure to what your audience cares about.

> "Sometimes you can make pages very feature based or you can make them customer based or use case based. So depending on your company like what might I don't know what makes sense like our page is fairly feature based because I like that because I think our audience is fairly like technical and so they they kind of know a lot of stuff and they they often look at the features and they like to to look at those kind of things. But if these people are not technical maybe they just want to know that their problems will be solved."

---

### Use Social Proof for Non-Technical Audiences

People want to see that others like them are using the product.

> "If these people are not technical maybe they just want to know that their problems will be solved and or something or that like other companies are other people like me are using it and like I should have their faces there saying something."

---

### Non-Technical Audiences Don't Need Amazing Websites

For non-technical users, trust and clarity matter more than impressive design.

> "These these kind of people who are not techsavvy, they're not necessarily looking at the most like amazing website."

> "Sometimes you can you don't have to try to do everything with the website. You could have like few things that you really want to do."

---

### Make Honest Claims

Don't overstate your credibility with claims you can't back up.

> "I don't know if it's like super honest to say like these are the most admired companies in the world. Um like the I think it's good that there are obviously seems like they're big companies, but I would say like in my mind there's other like I don't know Apple is very admired company or something. It's like I don't know if that statement is exactly true."

## Enterprise vs Consumer

### Enterprise Conversion Rates Work Differently

Don't optimize enterprise websites like consumer products. Different sales motions apply.

> "The reason the conversion is low because you're going after enterprises and it's like there there's probably no way to like increase that conversion flow like the conversion rate really because enterprises are not necessarily coming to websites and clicking buttons that much."

> "The conversion rate doesn't really matter because the if you sell if you do one deal that's probably going to be like a huge deal."

---

### Enterprise Sales Happen Offline

Website conversion isn't how you close enterprise deals. Sales, events, and outreach matter more.

> "You have to find those enterprises somewhere else like you have to do sales or some some kind of events or some kind of like dinners or some kind of ways to connect with the enterprise people because they are not often evaluating tools randomly."

---

### Enterprise Buyers Need Materials

Enterprise customers need extensive documentation, security pages, and case studies.

> "My understanding with enterprise buyers is that they they need a lot of almost like I think you need need a lot of materials. You need a lot of text. You need to check a lot of boxes. Yeah. You need a lot of customer stories. Like there's a lot of things they they want to see."

> "Maybe there's a specific category like maybe you have a specific page about security, maybe you have se specific page about something else. You could still when you email them you can link to those materials."

---

### Consider Self-Service for Higher Conversion

If you want more website conversions, offer a way for people to try without booking a demo.

> "If you do want to have more users or more people coming in, you probably should have a way for them to try it out themselves and and like kind of drop the demo."

---

### Demo Bots Need Guidance

AI demos should guide users toward successful interactions, not leave them guessing.

> "When she started talking, she basically just said, 'What do you want to talk about?' And I was like, 'I don't know.' Um, so it it might be better to start off with a prompt that could lead people to what um they want them to ask about."

> "I think steering people towards the conversation you want them to have with it that's most going to upsell them would probably make them more likely to want to book a demo."

**Bad Example:** [Giga ML](#case-study-giga-ml) — Voice agent opened with an open-ended question rather than guiding users.

---

### Match Demo Language to Customer Language

Use the words your customers actually search for.

> "What are these people looking for? So if I'm an enterprise buyer and I don't know the CEO tells me like we need to reduce the cost and like we want AI support like what do I type in... trying to think like what is the language these people are using and then try to put that language here so you are like aligning with what they're looking for."

# Case Studies

## Case Study: Linear (Evolution Example)

**Website:** Project management and issue tracking  
**Product:** Issue tracking evolved into building and planning platform  
**Context:** Used as example of how brands should evolve over time

### Early Stage (2019) — What Worked

- **Speed of launch:** "Something like we created in basically one day we didn't want to spend weeks or months trying to create a website"
- **Specific terminology:** "We wanted to use this issue tracking term... that's like something that the people we are looking for"
- **Strategic screenshot use:** "A little bit faded. So it's a little bit mysterious. So, it's like kind of like curious because in the end what we want people to do is put their email here"
- **Simple value props:** "Okay it's like fast because that's one thing we heard heard about a lot"
- **Team credibility:** "Here's the team and like we have some like this is not some random people but we have like built other things in the past"
- **Minimal pages:** "Very very simple. It's one page. There's no other pages... we don't have a about page and all kinds of we don't have a blog"
- **Clear goal:** "Let's just put something out there to capture people that um are potentially interested"

### Current Stage — What Worked

- **Evolved terminology:** "The the title is different because the product is actually doing more so so it's not just issue tracking but we think like there's this building part that has multiple different kinds of features"
- **Still clear language:** "We still like like to keep it pretty clear like here we say like issues projects and product road map so it's not like again it's the language that technical teams would use"
- **Matured visual style:** "We went more this kind of black and white we try to make it a little bit mature in a way like from the the purple from the past"
- **Purposeful effects:** "We we like doing this that they they kind of signal that we care about the experience and that's that's kind of like why we do this"
- **Separate detail pages:** "We now have specific sections for specific pages and sections for the the areas of the product. So you can go deeper in it"
- **Resisted feature overload:** "You've resisted the urge to make it overwhelming people with features"

### Key Lessons

- Spend minimal time on early websites — put effort into product
- Use specific language that attracts your ideal early users
- Build mystery with screenshots rather than showing everything
- Simple is better than polished when you're early
- Evolve your brand as your company and customers evolve
- Find one thing (speed) that matters most to early adopters

## Case Study: Sprites AI

**Website:** AI workflow builder  
**Product:** Custom AI workflow templates for various use cases

### What Worked

- Unique graphic style stands out
- Templates communicate variety of use cases

### What Failed

- **Unclear audience:** "I'm not exactly sure who is this for and what can I do with it... is it for designers is it for um engineers is it for like YouTubers"
- **Vague headline:** "Build custom AI workflows and streamline growth... streamline growth is well there's all kinds of growth like revenue growth and user growth there's marketing"
- **Distracting animation:** "There's this movement in the background and the movement on the cards... you're kind of kind of focused on the movement and like what's coming up next"
- **Graphics overshadow content:** "I paid so much attention to the graphics because they're so unique that I didn't even notice that it was like brand mood board generator, interview questions predictor"
- **Cookie banner pattern:** "This looks like a cookie banner in a way because that's a good point. It just kind of floats there"
- **No workflow explanation:** "I don't think the page um exactly describes how does it work"
- **Floating UI misplaced:** "I just wonder if if you just would put this prompter on the page here at the top and and it it could kind of like tell people, yeah, just like try it out and that way like you could communicate it better"

### Key Lessons

- Specify who the product is for
- Group use cases by audience type
- Avoid looping animations above the fold
- Don't let graphics compete with messaging
- Avoid UI patterns users are trained to ignore
- Show one clear example of how the workflow works
- Place interactive elements prominently on page, not floating

## Case Study: Giga ML

**Website:** Voice AI for enterprise  
**Product:** AI agents for enterprise customer support  
**Context:** Asked how to improve low conversion rate

### What Worked

- **Clean enterprise aesthetic:** "Quite quite clean and and uh I think it I don't know fits well for the for the market which is an enterprise"
- **Demo on page:** "This is a little small and it's hard to read this thing, but the animation did draw my attention to it"

### What Failed

- **Generic headline:** "AI agents for enterprise support is very generic"
- **Missing key product identifier:** "It is voice tune. You know, it's all about voice stuff, but like nothing up here says voice. So that's probably a missed opportunity to really target who your early adopters are going to be"
- **Redundant copy:** "Trusted by some of the most admired companies in the world... trusted by these great. It's like you don't need both of these"
- **Overstated claims:** "I don't know if it's like super honest to say like these are the most admired companies in the world"
- **Demo visibility:** "On the ORP it's it's hard to see the icon in it. It's it's kind of gray"
- **Demo guidance:** "She basically just said, 'What do you want to talk about?' And I was like, 'I don't know'"
- **Demo accuracy issues:** "She described it as S2 type 2, which you know, most people know it's like sock 2 type 2"
- **Missed sub-headline opportunity:** "The sub headline there, trusted by some of the most admired companies in the world, is a missed opportunity to literally describe what the product does"
- **Missing target specificity:** "Specifically who within enterprise should be the person that cares about this"

### Key Lessons

- Enterprise websites don't convert like consumer products
- Add self-service options for higher conversion
- Make demo elements more visible
- Guide demo interactions with suggested prompts
- Remove redundant trust statements
- Ensure demo accuracy — errors destroy trust
- Build supporting pages for security, features, etc.

## Case Study: Unreal Milk

**Website:** Lab-grown milk product  
**Product:** Cow milk created in a lab (not from cows)

### What Worked

- **Memorable design:** "I would remember this later on. The sides scrolling is unique and memorable. The graphics and illustrations are unique and memorable"
- **Original concept:** "This doesn't feel like it's copying anything that I've seen before. It feels like they from first principles came up with the concept"
- **Appropriate font choice:** "Their font choice is good here given the the style of it. It's more like handdrawn kind of environment"

## Case Study: Dropback

**Website:** Revenue sharing software for college athletics  
**Product:** Front office and back office operations for collegiate teams  
**Raised:** $1.6 million  
**Context:** Asked about landing page feeling chaotic

### What Worked

- **Clear value prop:** "I like that they are quite clear what they do and so I think that's good"
- **Video demo:** "It's quite short... it's it's showing some stuff so I think that's good... you are like clearly like demoing the product"
- **Feature specificity lower on page:** "They do get into the specific features... I think this helps to communicate"

### What Failed

- **Overwhelming hero:** "The bright colors and uh the gradients around the buttons and um you got like a moving grid behind like there is a lot going on here"
- **Distracting movement:** "The moving graphic is probably not meaningful because it's making hard to read the text"
- **Poor color contrast:** "The yellow color is a little bit again like I think there's not enough contrast"
- **Illegible graphics:** "This logo logo logo logo like documents I don't know what I don't know what would anyone think about that"
- **Constant animation:** "Basically every section is almost like moving and so I think it's yeah like there's again moving videos so I would maybe like try to reduce that"
- **Page too long:** "This page is very long too"
- **Wasted navigation:** "There's currently a lot of space in the navigation... these just link further down the page"
- **Text too small:** "Maybe this text could be even bigger. I I feel find it it's a little bit hard to read"

### Key Lessons

- Keep the hero simple — push details below the fold
- Remove or minimize moving backgrounds
- Yellow is hard to use — ensure sufficient contrast
- Don't make every section animated

- Consider audience-specific pages (coaches, programs)
- Trust users to scroll — don't cram everything at top
- For non-technical audiences, emphasize trust and social proof over features
