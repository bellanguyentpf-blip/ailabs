# Design Principles

## The Value of Design in Startups

### Design Is About Making Something People Want

Design isn't a superficial layer—it's the discipline of creating desirability, which is fundamental to startup success.

> "To build a product that people you know really really want I think you need kind of like three things to build a successful product in the company you need to have um something that you know desirable that people want you need need it to be viable so kind of like you can build a business out of it and you need it to be feasible you need to be uniquely positioned to actually build it."

> "If you think about these three things desirability feasibility and viability kind of has three overlapping circles and you kind of map them to more traditional disciplines is actually design and business and technology."

> "Design is really kind of like you know back to the YC ethos make something people want design is essentially figuring that."

---

### Good Design Is a Competitive Advantage (Especially Now)

With AI reimagining everything, companies that prioritize design have an outsized edge.

> "There was a recent tweet by uh Gary on uh president here at Y combinator and he says kind of like uh good design especially in early stage startups is a lost art."

> "Now is the moment where design can have the have like a huge outside outsized impact I feel I think companies that recognize the value of design especially right now um when everything is Shifting have a huge Edge."

---

### Hire Designers Early

For founders who aren't designers, bringing design talent in early provides enormous leverage.

> "Thinking about hiring um for design early on is is is a really uh important one."

> "To really kind of like uh amplify um their design I'm hiring uh talented designers relatively early on I think that's a huge leverage."

> "Tapping into you know networks such as you know y combinator or the designer fund here in Francisco um that have built out these networks kind of bringing designers more into tech companies early on I think um this is uh one way to to uh uh for Founders to really level up the design of their companies."

---

### Thoughtfulness Is Felt by Users

In a world obsessed with shipping speed, intentional design stands out.

> "It seems like the focus has become so much on speed rightly so shipping product building it we don't have time to be thoughtful about the design so whatever you know comes out is what comes out um and those that do put in the time to be thoughtful and focus on the things that really matter um it's felt I think by the end users and appreciated in a in a different way."

---

## Building in the Medium

### Designers Should Understand How Things Get Built

Being close to the actual implementation—whether through code or deep understanding of the medium—gives designers a huge advantage.

> "You should be comfortable in the medium that you're building in and if software is what you're building is made made out of code um but also you know pixels um being relatively close to like how the actual thing gets built gives you such a huge Advantage."

> "To have the ability to execute and build it yourself is like powerful... it's like the ultimate you know form of that."

---

### Design Is About Verbs, Not Just Nouns

In the AI age, design must move beyond static elements to consider behaviors and actions.

> "Especially now kind of like in the AI age where you know the thing that we design is less um kind of like the nouns kind of the buttons and the text fields and the sidebars but really think more about kind of like the verbs um you know autocomplete Auto suggest summarize send an agent uh to do something those are things that are kind of hard expressed just by drawing a rectangle within the rectangle within the rectangle."

> "You really need to start to think about time as an aspect in your designs... static prototypes often times kind of break down don't quite give you the feat back that you get with real data."

---

### Feel the Real Thing

Building in the actual medium lets you test whether a design truly works, not just whether it looks right. This connects looks, function, and implementation.

> "I don't necessarily need to draw it out um but I want to feel whether it also feels good and and the way we're building it whether you know that kind of works back to the sort of like design how it looks but how it works and also you know how it's built."

> "Whatever gives you sort of like ability to play with the real thing I think is what gives designers The Edge."

> "That's when you really need to build within the actual medium."

---

### Design and Engineering Shouldn't Be Entirely Separate

The separation between designing and building is a historical artifact that no longer serves us well.

> "If you were a sculptor and you just created the plans for the sculpture and then handed it off to somebody else to do the thing that would sound crazy right... so why would we think that kind of software design and soft engineering are these entirely separate disciplines."

## Developing Design Taste

### Learn By Doing

You can recognize good design, but you won't understand *why* until you try creating it yourself. Through practice, you also develop empathy for other designers' constraints.

> "First of all I would say uh just kind of do um learning by doing right um when you look at designs you often times kind of can tell this is a good design or this is not as good of a design this design works this design doesn't work as much but you don't really know why."

> "When you start doing you will probably produce bad designs uh at the beginning um but at least you can understand the material and you start to understand why a design works and doesn't work what are the constraints that a designer that created a design that maybe doesn't work as well um what what are the constraints that that Designer had right."

---

### Surround Yourself with Beauty

Taste is acquired through exposure. Curate your environment deliberately.

> "Surround yourself with beauty um surround yourself with like really nicely designed objects um can be physical but also can be the ergonomics of software."

> "If you don't tolerate and let in sort of like just that noise of like bad designed objects or non not designed objects... by the way like good design can also be cheap um like that's an aspect of kind of like good design production but surround yourself with um well-designed objects to sort of like absorb and and and sort of like develop a taste."

---

### Buy Fewer, Better Things

Quality over quantity trains your eye for good design.

> "Don't buy like a lot of things buy a few things that really like last and have high quality and then evaluating kind of the object like hm you know like why is this so pleasant to hold why is this uh you know work so well."

---

### Repairability Is a Hallmark of Good Design

Products that can be maintained and fixed demonstrate thoughtful design.

> "How can you repair things so repairability is also kind of like a Hallmark of good design."

---

### Don't Tolerate Bad Design

Developing taste requires actively distinguishing between what works and what doesn't.

> "Going through life with this lens and every interaction every door knob that you you know touch kind of thinking of it is this a pleasant experience um is this a reliable design is this a lasting design is it a design that gets better with age."

> "By surrounding yourself and not tolerating uh kind of bad design um is not about kind of being snobby it's more about uh separating like even even thinking about the design Dimension and separating something from being well-designed and not well-designed."

---

### Taste Can't Be Taught, But Can Be Acquired

You develop taste through immersion and practice, not instruction.

> "You can't maybe learn um taste but you can certainly kind of like acquire it."

---

### Read the Design Classics

Books distill deep knowledge that accelerates your understanding.

> "Read some design books um there's so much deep knowledge uh in some of these Classics."

**Recommended Books:**

1. **Grid Systems** — "What taught me all about text and layout." *Fun fact: Cron's signature orange (#FF470) was inspired by the cover of this iconic book.*
2. **The Elements of Typographic Style** — "It kind of taught me everything um about type uh not just kind of choosing a font but how to use type as a graphic and as an interface element."
3. **The Design of Everyday Things** — "It's a classic it's the the the the book with the cover that has a teapot where the spout comes on at the wrong wrong wrong end kind of showing you about usability. Main takeaway from that one actually is um to convey the importance of conveying uh the mental model of the uh apparatus or software that you built to the user through through UI through design... this is a tried and truee classic."

---

### Once You See Design, You See It Everywhere

Understanding design changes how you perceive the world—it's both a gift and a burden.

> "You really start to kind of almost see the world through sort of like uh you know reading the world kind of through like Matrix um you see another dimension to the world when you start kind of understanding design."

> "Once you sort of like you know you kind of know about kerning and ligatures and microt typography line spacing Etc you see the world in a completely different way it's kind of like a little bit of blessing and the curse but extremely powerful."

## The Design Process

### Start with User Problems

Before sketching anything, understand what users need.

> "You oftentimes kind of first talk to users right um You may have some intuition but you really want to in the end uh solve user problems so that's where where it really starts right Intuition or going of like user problems."

> "It really then comes down to what user problem are you solving and is it sort of like an Innovative approach um to to solving it."

---

### Start with Sketching on Paper

Paper is the ideal medium for early ideation because it's both constrained and unconstrained. Use whatever captures the idea fastest.

> "Typically kind of for me starts um uh sketching... when you kind of want to quickly visualize something like very quickly move from insight to capture that Insight um I still found it most efficiently to just quickly chot it down on a piece of paper."

> "That's what I still kind of like use my sketchbooks for still just sketching a ton of interface ideas and kind of whatever is easiest for you to to quickly capture the idea I think that's most important."

> "One of the reasons why um I think sketching on paper is so powerful is because um the medium of paper affords you to do anything um you don't have any constraints at that point yet once you start to get into like design tool or code you start to kind of get like dragged down by so like the constraints of the medium."

---

### Paper Focuses Without Limiting

The page provides just enough structure to capture ideas while allowing complete creative freedom.

> "Paper is this super flexible like it gives you just enough sort of like boundary right it kind of the page focuses you within this sort of like you know within the page to kind of get an idea out of your mind it's not this infinite thing so the constraint there is really good but then within the page it's completely unconstrained like I can draw anything right you're not limited to lines and rectangles and text."

---

### Paper Is Universally Approachable

Unlike design tools, sketching excludes no one—and sketches can easily be shared to increase fidelity.

> "Paper is super approachable for non-designers everyone can like doodle right it's super approachable it's very flexible and you can really imagine uh anything on the page."

> "You can take it snap a picture or whatever send it to an engineer uh or or um a designer to can like increase the Fidelity of it um and so or upload it to AI now."

---

### Move to High Fidelity Quickly

Don't linger in low-fidelity—get to the real thing as fast as possible. Different paths work for different people. If you know what good looks like, you can skip intermediate steps.

> "Moving pretty quickly from capturing that sort of like visual insight and give it some form to starting to play with it um as High Fidelity as possible."

> "For some people this may kind of be you know uh maybe taking a photo of the sketch and putting it in figma and starting to kind of like you know wireframing it out or directly kind of more Pixel Perfect designs."

> "What I sometimes really like to do is also just kind of take the sketch have it on the table and then um jump into like a code editor and already kind of try try to start to assemble um sort of like the UI to start to feel it because I kind of like know what good looks like um I don't necessarily need to draw it out."

---

### AI Makes Sketching More Valuable, Not Less

The assumption that we should "start digital first" is being upended. Sketches can now skip straight to implementation, making early ideation even more important.

> "For a while it maybe kind of seemed that sketching is um is like clearly we should just start this digital first right but what if now you know kind of like the value of a sketch actually becomes very important again when you can skip that entire step."

> "You uh kind of chot down an idea you quickly sketch it out you you take a picture of it you upload it into design tool that then turns it into the real deal you can skip everything in between."

## Balancing Innovation & Familiarity

### Respect Platform Norms

Users have expectations from years of computing. Don't reinvent what works.

> "You kind of start to develop an intuition especially kind on just just interaction design like what works what doesn't what's consistent with platform norms."

> "Respecting kind of like you know user expectations and standards that they you know are used to from years of computing not Reinventing the wheel."

> "This goes down to like when should a field you know blur or Focus or when should a drop- down uh appear when you focus the field like how how does it interact with text with the keyboard."

---

### Push Boundaries While Staying Familiar

Great design innovates without alienating.

> "That's kind of where I think this dance this elegant dance um as a designer as a builder kind of comes in to to really start to build something something that just feels right feels fun feels novel um but still kind of familiar."

---

### "Like" Isn't a Design Word

Design judgment should be about function, not preference.

> "I always kind of say like like is not a design word it either works or doesn't work."

---

### Users Are the Ultimate Judge

Your initial assessment matters, but real validation comes from putting the design in front of users.

> "That initial assessment might kind of lie with the designer but then pretty quickly because you built the thing or like at least a prototype of it you can actually distribute it um over the web or over you know just kind of show someone uh on a laptop on your prototype and let them interact with it and ultimately I think users are the ultimate judge whether a design works or doesn't work."

# Key Concepts

## The Design-Business-Technology Triad

Successful products require the overlap of three elements, each mapped to a discipline:

| Element | Question | Discipline |
|---------|----------|------------|
| **Desirability** | Do people want it? | Design |
| **Viability** | Can you build a business? | Business |
| **Feasibility** | Can you actually build it? | Technology |

> "Design is really kind of like you know back to the YC ethos make something people want design is essentially figuring that."

---

## Art vs. Design

Design has evolved from an artistic pursuit to a problem-solving discipline.

> "Back in the day um design was much closer kind of to an artistic Pursuit right where art is more about kind of like asking questions um whereas design these days is more actually about kind of problem solving and answering um questions they're learing Solutions basically."

> "That probably happened sometimes during the industrialization is is my guess um where design kind of moved closer to uh be part of sort of like the problem solving you know engineering kind of apparatus."

---

## Designers Now Control the Means of Production

For the first time in history, designers can build what they envision.

> "Back then in the industrialization designers didn't necessarily control the means of production right um and so that is really changing now where designers can essentially through software through code can actually be really close to building the object right um versus kind of like you know back in the day maybe it's furniture or or uh like like other objects."

> "I think that's what makes uh this moment in time so interesting for designers to build companies and build products."

---

## Everything Is Designed

Design isn't optional—it's inherent in everything. The question is whether it's intentional.

> "Once I realized like everything is designed whether it's proactively designed or not like everything is designed even like kind of accidental design right every pen every table everything is designed and even things that we don't think of like a designer so far like everything has like someone that made it now you can kind of consciously made it better."

> "Everything is designed the question is whether it was intentional or not and whether thought went into it or not."

This realization is empowering—you can influence the world around you:

> "I think this is a famous Steve Steve Jobs quote like there's people around me are not not that much smarter than me you know so you mean like I can actually influence all of this and that's when I was like aha like naturally I need to become a designer."

---

## Design Beyond Aesthetics

Design encompasses looks, function, *and* implementation.

> "Design is not just kind of how it looks but design is famously you know how it works I would even take this a step further I would say design is how it looks how it works but design is how it's built you can feel it end to end."

> "The material that is used to create an object the piece of software the framework whatever like to me that is part of the design."

This includes:
- Loading states
- Latencies
- Technology choices
- Framework decisions

---

## Design That Gets Better with Age

Well-designed products improve through use, developing a kind of patina.

> "Is it a design that gets better with age um and I don't just mean kind of like you know physical patina but also in software um something that gets better over time by you you know the the interface getting adaptive for example making it more obvious how I use it muscle memory keyboard shortcuts kind of peeling the onion of of of sort of like you know interfaces."

## Key Takeaways for Founders

1. **Designers should consider founding companies** — They're uniquely positioned to create desirable products
2. **Technical founders should develop design sensibility** — It's one of the most important skills for building next-generation companies
3. **Get close to the medium** — Whether through coding or deep collaboration, understanding implementation makes design better
4. **Start with user problems, then sketch on paper** — Move quickly from insight to capture
5. **Move to high fidelity fast** — Don't get stuck in wireframes; feel the real thing
6. **Surround yourself with well-designed things** — Taste is absorbed, not taught
7. **Everything is designed** — Choose to do it intentionally
8. **Design is how it looks, works, AND is built** — End-to-end quality matters
9. **Hire designers early** — Tap into networks like YC or Designer Fund for talent
10. **Users are the ultimate judge** — Get prototypes in front of people quickly

> "My main message kind of for the audience would just be you know uh to encourage more designers to become Founders um it's been an amazing journey for me um partially also through y combinator um and then for existing Founders um to kind of like develop uh sort like a this design mind because it's going to be one of the most important skill sets through you build like the huge Next Generation companies."
