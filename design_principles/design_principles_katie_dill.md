# Design Principles

## Messaging & Clarity

### Start with Concrete Value, Not Abstract Concepts

Don't make users scroll through atmospheric content to understand what you do. Lead with your value proposition.

> "Blood work and personalized insights to help you live healthier and longer—like I don't know why they didn't just start the site here. That's very descriptive. It like helps me understand a little bit about why I want this thing."

**Bad Example:** [Meo Health](#case-study-meo-health) — Started with vague "predict your future health" messaging and atmospheric video before explaining the actual product.

---

### Let Users Skim Your Headlines

Many visitors only scan headlines. Ensure they tell a complete story on their own.

> "There's a couple different reads in the website right... a lot of people just scan and so if I was just scanning if I just read the headlines would I understand it."

**Good Example:** [SigNoz](#case-study-signoz) — "They're getting a lot more descriptive up front... what this is, what's it for, a little bit about kind of relaying a bit about who it's for."

---

### Explain What You Are, Who It's For, and What Problem You Solve

Every landing page must clearly communicate these three things immediately.

> "I'm a little confused who this is for, like who the user is and who they're trying to attract... like why should I trust this company, like what is, how do you know what you're talking about."

**Bad Example:** [Amino Analytica](#case-study-amino-analytica) — Visual design was fun but failed to communicate who the product was for or what problem it solved.

---

### Use Your Customers' Language for Headlines

Ask your users how they describe your product—that's likely how potential customers will think about it too.

> "A great way to figure out what should be your headline is to just ask your users, like how would you describe our product, because chances are the way your users think about your product and would describe it is the way that other users potentially would too."

**Needs Improvement:** [Taive](#case-study-taive) — "Give your TVs superpowers" was unclear; the subline "enhance live TV in restaurants to increase revenue" was much more descriptive.

---

### Don't Bold the Negative Things

When users skim, they read headlines and bold text. Make sure those emphasize your value, not problems.

> "Don't bold the negative things, right? Like so now I'm just skimming and I'm reading 'trial and error,' 'random mutations,' and I don't know if that's what you want me to take away. You want me to take away the value."

**Bad Example:** [Amino Analytica](#case-study-amino-analytica) — Bold text emphasized negative concepts rather than the product's benefits.

---

## Visual Design & Polish

### Every Pixel Should Add Value

Decorative elements that don't communicate anything meaningful are just noise.

> "One question you could always ask yourself is like, is every one of those pixels adding value? Like did you... did they need that line? These are the trusted companies—like I already, I get that just seeing it, right? Like I don't know if I need that information there too, so that you can focus on more of the core things you're trying to explain."

**Bad Example:** [SigNoz](#case-study-signoz) — Extra labels like "these are the trusted companies" added no value when the logos were self-explanatory.

---

### Align Visuals with Brand Attributes

Define what adjectives you want people to feel before designing, then ensure visuals communicate those attributes.

> "A really great practice is like write down on a piece of paper as you're going into your website design—what adjectives do we want people to feel? What is our brand? What is that energy we have? Like so for example at Stripe, like we purposely are about exuberant color because we're about positivity and we're about progress and we want to look optimistically at the world and so that hopefully communicates through what we do."

**Good Example:** Stripe — Uses exuberant color to communicate positivity and progress.

**Needs Improvement:** [Metriport](#case-study-metriport) — Abstract blob animation didn't communicate anything meaningful about the brand or product.

---

### Decorative Elements Must Communicate Something

Every visual element should reinforce your brand message, not just fill space.

> "What is the value of this thing? Is it communicating, you know, any attribute of which you are trying to relay?... Does it communicate anything to you? No."

> "Do you want to show your technical prowess with this? I would not say that is kind of communicating there."

**Bad Example:** [Metriport](#case-study-metriport) — Abstract line animation was cut off awkwardly and didn't communicate technical capability or any brand attribute.

---

### Video Thumbnails Must Add Value

If you include a video, make sure the thumbnail screenshot is useful even if users never click play.

> "It's not great when people include a video and it's just like, you know, nothing—it's like no value whatsoever—because I might not always click on the video, getting any value out of the thumbnail."

> "If you're going to include a video, make sure that screenshot is adding value even if I don't tap on it."

**Good Example:** [SigNoz](#case-study-signoz) — "The screenshot is useful... I can tell by the little video thing but the screenshot is useful."

**Bad Example:** [Metriport](#case-study-metriport) — "The screenshot is just what we just saw, so that's no new value added."

---

### Watch the Small Details

Inconsistent capitalization, unintentional shadows, and misaligned elements erode trust.

> "Be super careful about, you know, the details in your grammar and your... your casing. So 'give your TV superpowers'—I don't know why 'superpowers' is capitalized. It's like a kind of hybrid between title case and sentence case. So sticking to that consistently."

> "Every one of those little things, you know, it might just think like 'oh, that's not a big deal, the user could still read it,' but it's a little point of friction and again it's something that erodes trust—like if you're not on top of that detail, what other detail are you not on top of?"

**Bad Example:** [Taive](#case-study-taive) — Mixed "Get Started" (title case) with "learn more" (sentence case); "superpowers" randomly capitalized.

**Bad Example:** [Meo Health](#case-study-meo-health) — "There's also a little funky drop shadow under that measure button... that's just like a tiny little detail but that, you know, I don't think was intentional."

---

### Fun Shouldn't Sacrifice Usability

You can be playful in B2B, but not at the cost of readability and comprehension.

> "Absolutely you can be fun in a B2B world... we're designing for humans and humans want to have a little joy in their day. So I really really appreciate that. I think there's like a lot of fun here. I think the color is energetic... What I think you have to be super careful about is that at what point does it start eroding usability."

> "The logo is difficult to read... there's a lot going on... I'm not actually learning what the product is and like who is it for and what is the problem that it's solving. So I would say don't lose the fun but definitely go back to make it more effective and how well it communicates."

**Bad Example:** [Amino Analytica](#case-study-amino-analytica) — Creative design undermined readability with hard-to-read logo and confusing typography choices.

---

## Information Hierarchy

### Establish Clear CTA Hierarchy

Not all calls-to-action are equal. Make your primary action most prominent and secondary actions visually subordinate.

> "It's a good idea to make your CTAs clear, but those are really overpowering. And I also think you should think about the hierarchy of your CTAs—so are those, you know, the most important thing, or for example that, you know, that 'book demo' CTA up here is probably more important, so you might want to think about making these more like secondary links."

**Bad Example:** [Metriport](#case-study-metriport) — "Learn more" buttons on product cards were as prominent as the main "Book Demo" CTA.

---

### Don't Make Users Click to Get Core Information

People are less likely to click than scroll. Put essential information on the page, not behind buttons.

> "People are less likely to click something than they are to scroll. And so I would definitely consider bringing that insight here and helping people understand, like, why should I trust Amino Analytica for all of my protein needs."

**Bad Example:** [Amino Analytica](#case-study-amino-analytica) — "There's not a lot here and so they're making me have to hit a button to go and actually get that insight."

---

### Show Product Screenshots Prominently

Visitors want to see what they're buying. Lead with real product visuals.

> "I also really love the product shot right off the bat. I get a sense of like, this is a thing I can buy, this is what it would be like."

> "Each of these value propositions are complemented with an image—it helps me get a sense of the product and it helps me better understand what I would be getting."

**Good Example:** [SigNoz](#case-study-signoz) — Led with product screenshot and complemented each value proposition with relevant imagery.

**Bad Example:** [Meo Health](#case-study-meo-health) — "Kind of small, can I really understand what's going on here."

---

### Feature Text Shouldn't Compete with Product Screenshots

When your product involves text-heavy interfaces (code, logs), simplify screenshots so they don't compete with explanatory copy.

> "Part of the product here is code and logs which have a lot of text in them, and when you try to show that in screenshots it competes with the actual text that you're trying to communicate what's happening... Potentially, like, can you make that smaller, or can you abstract it a little bit to give some of the sense of it but without being too busy."

**Needs Improvement:** [SigNoz](#case-study-signoz) — Text in product screenshots competed with marketing copy.

## User Experience & Interaction

### Never Hijack the Scroll

Taking control of scrolling frustrates users and increases bounce rates. Users are impatient—your job is to earn their attention at every step.

> "I hate when sites hijack the scroll."

> "When you look at web design, you kind of have to think about it as that they want to leave—like TikTok is calling, they've got other things to do—and so what your job is is to like show them why they should be there and help keeping them from every step of the way. A lot of scrolls like that, you know, it's just like, I don't got time for that."

**Bad Example:** [Meo Health](#case-study-meo-health) — Scroll hijacking with slow-moving video required excessive scrolling to progress.

---

### Don't Tell Users How to Use Your Website

If you need instructions, your design isn't intuitive enough.

> "Never a good sign if you have to tell them how to use it, like 'scroll to'... you should scroll. Yeah, exactly. Like that should be more intuitive—and it's a website, so like people should know what to do, but it was not clear there."

**Bad Example:** [Meo Health](#case-study-meo-health) — "Scroll to explore" instruction indicated unclear navigation.

---

### Make Visual Affordances Match Behavior

If something looks scrollable or clickable, it should behave that way.

> "Your first reaction was like this should scroll, which like it was communicating well, right? It was like hanging off the edge... I thought that, but yeah, I would have as well thought to scroll."

**Needs Improvement:** [Meo Health](#case-study-meo-health) — Carousel elements suggested horizontal scrolling but required clicking.

---

### Test on Multiple Device Sizes

Always check how your design performs on tablets and mobile phones.

> "Make your window smaller—how does it work on a mobile device? We should always be thinking about when you're assessing it, it's like, what if I'm looking at it on a tablet? What if I'm looking at on my mobile phone? Like does it, you know, also condense well and tell that story well? Because your users oftentimes are on the go when they're coming to this kind of environment."

**Good Example:** [Taive](#case-study-taive) — "Seems like it performed well here" when tested at smaller screen sizes.

---

### Map and Optimize the Full User Journey

Don't optimize landing pages in isolation. Consider what brought users there and what happens after they convert.

> "They should think about the journey of how did somebody even get here. What was the wording of the thing they saw that got them here, right? And then yeah, what happens when they hit that button—how easy is it for them to sign up and get going."

> "One really great thing for any organization to do is just like map out the user journey—like each point, what is happening and how good is that point, is it actually working or not—and then just improve it. Because sometimes it's misleading, like this thing is great but the thing right before it tells a totally different story, and that's where your problem is."

**General Advice:** [SigNoz](#case-study-signoz) — Rather than just optimizing the landing page, examine the entire journey from ad to conversion.

---

## Brand & Trust

### First Impressions Set the Emotional Tone

Consider what vibe your design communicates immediately—is it aligned with your brand promise?

> "One of the things that immediately react to this is you think about what is your first impression and how is this brand communicating how they think you should feel. I would say that this has a vibe that is a little bit more foreboding about my future health as opposed to uplifting, and I don't know if that's intentional."

**Bad Example:** [Meo Health](#case-study-meo-health) — Dark, moody aesthetic felt foreboding for a health optimization product.

---

### Show Social Proof with Recognizable Logos

Customer logos build trust, especially when visitors recognize them.

> "We get a bunch of logos here and I recognize a bunch of these logos, which is good—so that helps build trust and credibility."

**Good Example:** [SigNoz](#case-study-signoz) — Displayed recognizable customer logos to establish credibility.

**Good Example:** [Taive](#case-study-taive) — "Their logos kind of communicate this too, like Ferg Sports Bar and... Most Cantina—like feels like restaurants... so it feels like, oh, your customers are restaurant owners like me."

---

### Portray Your Actual Customer

Details in mockups and examples should reflect your real target audience, not contradict it.

> "It's interesting, so they're showing an example that this person is 21 years old. Is that their customer? So you should think about every one of those details—is that like, am I portraying, you know, who that I'm actually talking to?"

> "There was like a picture of an older man and then yet it's telling me I'm a 21-year-old, so I'm not really, you know, understanding: is this for me?"

**Bad Example:** [Meo Health](#case-study-meo-health) — Imagery showed older adults while UI mockup showed a 21-year-old user, creating confusion about target audience.

---

### Logo Design Should Align with Brand Promise

Your logo is part of your message. Ensure it communicates what you want.

> "In the logo... it looks like it's a TV that is cut in half. Is that the communication that you want to have—like you're breaking TVs, you know, you're ripping them apart, you're slashing them? Seems like it doesn't correlate as much with what they're trying to accomplish with the actual brand."

> "The name is hard to understand or read, and then how the two sit together—it looks like the word is sitting kind of below the... actual icon, and that also kind of feels unbalanced. So I think there's some iteration that you could do on the logo."

**Bad Example:** [Taive](#case-study-taive) — Logo looked like a slashed/broken TV, contradicting the product's value proposition.

---

### Explain Your Process

Users want to understand how things work before committing.

> "It was great that they did show the steps of how this works. I think people do want to internalize, like, well what does this work and how is that going to make me work."

**Good Example:** [Meo Health](#case-study-meo-health) — "How it works" section showing steps was valuable (though incomplete—missing "take the test" step).

---

### Listen to Users Talk Through Your Site

The best conversion optimization comes from watching real users interact with your site.

> "My guess is that the key thing for them to do if they haven't done it already is to listen to users doing what we do—sitting down, looking at it for the first time, and then just like here, user have a user talk out loud and what their reactions are."

**General Advice:** [SigNoz](#case-study-signoz) — User research recommended over pure visual optimization for improving conversion rates.

# Case Studies

## Case Study: Meo Health

**Website:** Blood work and health insights platform  
**Product:** Personalized health predictions and testing to help users live healthier and longer

**Founder Question:** "We'd love to increase conversions while telling a story with compelling visuals and animations. How do we balance both?"

### What Worked

- **Descriptive value proposition:** "Blood work and personalized insights to help you live healthier and longer—like I don't know why they didn't just start the site here. That's very descriptive"
- **Process explanation:** "It was great that they did show the steps of how this works. I think people do want to internalize, like, well what does this work"
- **Product shots:** "Love product shots" (though noted they could be larger)
- **Persistent CTA:** "The CTA is there, it comes as I go down the page... making sure that it's never hard to find the next action"

### What Failed

- **Foreboding atmosphere:** "This has a vibe that is a little bit more foreboding about my future health as opposed to uplifting"
- **Excessive scrolling required:** "That's a lot of scrolls... they've taken over the screen. I hate when sites hijack the scroll"
- **Instruction-dependent navigation:** "Never a good sign if you have to tell them how to use it, like 'scroll to'... that should be more intuitive"
- **Wasted above-the-fold space:** "There actually isn't a lot of information here and they're telling you scroll to explore and they're not really giving you much above the fold"
- **Too much storytelling before value:** "There was just too much of that storytelling at first and I think it wasn't for any value—like I didn't necessarily take away from that video"
- **Inconsistent user portrayal:** "A picture of an older man and then yet it's telling me I'm a 21-year-old, so I'm not really understanding: is this for me"
- **Incomplete process steps:** "Schedule test, receive results and action plan, take action and test again—is schedule test the thing, or is it take the test?... Yeah, I guess you need to take the test. That's kind of missing"
- **Unintentional visual bugs:** "There's also a little funky drop shadow under that measure button... that, you know, I don't think was intentional"
- **Small product shots:** "Kind of small, can I really understand what's going on here"

### Key Lessons

- Start with concrete product description, not atmospheric video
- Don't hijack scrolling; respect user time and attention ("TikTok is calling, they've got other things to do")
- Make navigation intuitive—if you need instructions, redesign
- Ensure all imagery consistently represents your actual target customer
- Include all steps in process explanations
- Make product screenshots large enough to be meaningful
- Match visual mood to brand promise (uplifting for health products)
- **Answer to founder question:** "There was just too much of that storytelling at first and I think it wasn't for any value... starting with something more concrete around what is this and who is it for is probably a better place to start"

## Case Study: SigNoz

**Website:** Observability platform  
**Product:** OpenTelemetry native logs, metrics, and traces in a single pane

**Founder Question:** "How can we improve the landing design to improve click-through rate, i.e., the percent of people who click 'Try SigNoz Cloud' from our landing page?"

### What Worked

- **Clear description up front:** "They're getting a lot more descriptive up front... what this is, what's it for, a little bit about kind of relaying a bit about who it's for. I think that that is strong"
- **Product shot immediately visible:** "I also really love the product shot right off the bat. I get a sense of like, this is a thing I can buy, this is what it would be like"
- **Useful video thumbnail:** "The screenshot is useful. It's not great when people include a video and it's just like, you know, nothing"
- **Recognizable customer logos:** "A bunch of logos here and I recognize a bunch of these logos, which is good—so that helps build trust and credibility"
- **Value props with images:** "Each of these value propositions are complemented with an image—it helps me get a sense of the product and it helps me better understand what I would be getting"
- **Clear CTAs:** "I think, you know, the CTAs—they're clear what they do... it's not just like 'just start it,' it tells you like okay you can try it"
- **Product shown prominently:** "I think they're really doing a great job by showing the product right off the bat"

### What Failed

- **Too many type sizes:** "There also was a lot of different type sizes going on here... there's a lot to take in"
- **Unnecessary labels:** "Is every one of those pixels adding value? Like did they need that line? These are the trusted companies—like I already, I get that just seeing it"
- **Key features hard to read:** "These are kind of small—I feel like maybe this is important, like the core features of what they do... but it's kind of hard to read that and I probably would have skipped over it"
- **Overwhelming information density:** "There's a lot going on here... that feels a little overwhelming and makes it harder to dig in and understand exactly what I'm looking at"
- **Text-heavy screenshots:** "Part of the product here is code and logs which have a lot of text in them, and when you try to show that in screenshots it competes with the actual text"
- **Confusing tagline:** "Built for developers, crafted by humans—are developers not humans? Sounds like maybe they don't think so"

### Conversion Rate Advice

> "My guess is that the key thing for them to do if they haven't done it already is to listen to users doing what we do—sitting down, looking at it for the first time, and then just like here, have a user talk out loud and what their reactions are."

> "From a pure visual perspective, like it's clear there are buttons and clear call to action. So my hunch is the best way to improve conversion rates is one, in how you are communicating the value of the product and showing it off—whether it's screenshots, videos—or the type of people that are on the page, to make sure that they're actually the right targeted users."

### Key Lessons

- Show product immediately—don't make users hunt for what it looks like
- Video thumbnails should stand alone as useful images
- Remove redundant labels that add noise without value
- Establish clear visual hierarchy with consistent type sizing
- Abstract text-heavy product interfaces in screenshots to reduce competition with marketing copy
- Watch for awkward phrasing that could confuse or amuse users unintentionally
- For conversion optimization, watch real users navigate your site rather than guessing
- Consider the full journey: "What was the wording of the thing they saw that got them here? What happens when they hit that button? How easy is it for them to sign up and get going?"
- Consider whether "try for free" or "get going with a trial" messaging fits your value exchange model

## Case Study: Taive

**Website:** TV enhancement platform for restaurants  
**Product:** Software that enhances live TV in restaurants to increase revenue and save time

### What Worked

- **Specific value claims:** "Our average partner makes 2K per location a year, no cost, cancel anytime" — concrete benefits visible immediately
- **Clear value proposition visible:** "They are trying to direct that... it's a lot to take in but I do think that it's powerful that they are kind of communicating some of these aspects"
- **Strong subline:** "Enhance live TV in restaurants to increase revenue—now it's very clear to me, like, oh, I own a restaurant, this product is for me"
- **Relevant social proof:** "Their logos kind of communicate this too, like Ferg Sports Bar and... Most Cantina—like feels like restaurants... so it feels like, oh, your customers are restaurant owners like me"
- **Mobile responsive:** "Seems like it performed well here" when tested at smaller screen sizes

### What Failed

- **Unclear headline:** "Give your TVs superpowers—I don't know that that's how their users think about this product"
- **Visual overload:** "There is a lot going on here... there's a moving image behind it, for example, they've got glow on the buttons, these arrows"
- **Meaningless arrows:** "Why does this one go up, when does this one go down?... I don't think those are adding any value"
- **Unnecessary nav styling:** "Up here in the nav, you know, these color boxes around... I'm not sure that's necessarily an advantage"
- **Confusing logo:** "It looks like it's a TV that is cut in half. Is that the communication that you want to have—like you're breaking TVs"
- **Unbalanced logo layout:** "The word is sitting kind of below the actual icon, and that also kind of feels unbalanced"
- **Hard to read brand name:** "The name is hard to understand or read"
- **Inconsistent capitalization:** "I don't know why 'superpowers' is capitalized... it's like a kind of hybrid between title case and sentence case"
- **Mixed CTA casing:** "'Get Started,' for example, is title case, 'learn more' is sentence case—we you should always be consistent"
- **Noise from labels:** "Is 'trusted by the best' helping you right now? No, it's kind of just like adding a little noise to the page"

### Key Lessons

- Use customer language for headlines—ask users how they describe your product
- Remove decorative elements that don't add value
- Maintain strict consistency in capitalization and grammar
- Ensure logo communicates your value proposition, not the opposite
- Every visual element should have a purpose
- Test responsive design across device sizes

## Case Study: Metriport

**Website:** Healthcare data platform  
**Product:** Universal API for healthcare data—comprehensive patient medical data in seconds

### What Worked

- **Clear value proposition:** "Universal API for healthcare data—okay, that's pretty clear"
- **Compelling time savings:** "Get comprehensive patient medical data in seconds not weeks"
- **Customer logos:** "Trusted by leaders building the future of healthcare—so I assume these are customers"
- **Value prop clarity (founder question answered):** "Is our value prop clear enough?... Yeah, I understand what they do from this right out of the gate, so I think they nailed that"
- **Visual interest acknowledged:** "I think it is a good thing to introduce some visual interest... it's a good thing to bring dynamic attributes into the site. It makes it feel a little bit more alive... beauty is engaging, people want to be among beautiful things, so I don't think that's ever wrong"

### What Failed

- **Abstract animation doesn't communicate:** "What is the value of this thing? Is it communicating, you know, any attribute of which you are trying to relay?... Does it communicate anything to you? No"
- **Awkward visual cutoff:** "It also really bothers me that it's kind of cut off there at the end—like those lines really want to go outside the frame and they do not"
- **Unreadable elements under hero:** "I can't tell what these are under here... not really adding a lot of value"
- **Overpowering secondary CTAs:** "Those are really overpowering. And I also think you should think about the hierarchy of your CTAs"
- **Busy product cards:** "It's getting kind of busy here... there's a lot going on in these cards plus those 'learn more' buttons"
- **Useless video thumbnail:** "The screenshot is just what we just saw, so that's no new value added. I think if you're going to include a video, make sure that screenshot is adding value even if I don't tap on it"

### Key Lessons

- Decorative animations should communicate brand attributes, not just fill space
- Establish clear CTA hierarchy—primary actions most prominent
- Video thumbnails must provide value as standalone images
- Clean up visual clutter in product cards
- Ensure all visual elements render completely and intentionally

## Case Study: Amino Analytica

**Website:** Protein design platform  
**Product:** Accelerated protein design for B2B engineering applications

**Founder Question:** "Does a B2C-style website—one that looks fun, personable, flashy—work for traditional corporate B2B engineering company, or does it come across as unserious?"

### What Worked

- **Fun, energetic approach validated:** "Absolutely you can be fun in a B2B world... we're designing for humans and humans want to have a little joy in their day. So I really really appreciate that"
- **Colorful, energetic palette:** "I think the color is energetic and I think that there's some value in that"
- **Creative visual approach:** "I love what they're trying to do here"

### What Failed

- **Logo difficult to read:** "The logo is difficult to read"
- **Typography undermines usability:** "There's a lot going on... why does 'adaptation' have to be bigger than 'accelerated'... the different typefaces that are used—I don't know if we're actually making it useful. It's like actually kind of harder to read"
- **Unclear audience and problem:** "I'm not actually learning what the product is and like who is it for and what is the problem that it's solving"
- **Requires clicks for essential info:** "There's not a lot here and so they're making me have to hit a button to go and actually get that insight... people are less likely to click something than they are to scroll"
- **No credibility signals:** "Why should I trust this company, like what is, how do you know what you're talking about"
- **Bold text emphasizes negatives:** "Don't bold the negative things... now I'm just skimming and I'm reading 'trial and error,' 'random mutations,' and I don't know if that's what you want me to take away"
- **No clear call to action:** "There's no call to action here... they're directing people to [Launch YC] and then there's a 'learn more' button down here"
- **Distracting motion:** "It moves... there's a lot going on—like this actually is kind of like radiating in some way"

### Key Lessons

- Fun is welcome in B2B, but usability comes first
- Don't sacrifice readability for creativity
- Put trust-building information on the page, not behind clicks
- Bold and emphasize your value, not the problems you solve
- Have a clear primary call to action
- Ensure your logo is readable
- Limit motion that distracts from comprehension
