# Design Principles

## The 5-Second Rule

### Capture Attention Immediately or Lose the Visitor

You have a tiny window to make an impression. Everything about your above-the-fold experience must be optimized for instant comprehension.

> "When someone clicks over to your website this is how long you have to make a great first impression... if you can't capture their attention in that time it doesn't matter how good the rest of your website is."

> "In the context of only having a few seconds... if we got 5 seconds to make a decision on if we're going to stick around if the page hasn't even loaded in at that point... we're probably gone."

**Good Example:** [Capacity](#case-study-capacity) — "It seemed pretty straightforward... within 5 seconds we easily read that and were able to digest the illustration."

**Bad Example:** [Cloud Thread](#case-study-cloud-thread) — "I think I would have given up and hit the back button by this point."

---

### Make the Takeaway Memorable

After 5 seconds, visitors should be able to recall what your product does.

> "What's your takeaway what do you remember from that?"

**Good Example:** [Bottomless](#case-study-bottomless) — "We remembered the different tea, kombucha, bananas, peanut butter and that's with us even after just few seconds."

**Bad Example:** [Artisan AI](#case-study-artisan-ai) — "I know it's an AI assistant but it didn't say that... I'm like having to think about like what are they trying to tell me."

---

## Messaging & Clarity

### One Headline, One Byline, One CTA

Simplify your above-the-fold content to a single, clear message. Multiple headlines create confusion about where to focus.

> "They could probably summarize the entire top of fold into one headline one byline and one call to action."

> "We have two equal weight columns and I think what that tells the user here is that both are equally important and that you should wait both the same."

**Bad Example:** [Artisan AI](#case-study-artisan-ai) — "I didn't really know the hierarchy... I didn't know where to focus on two headlines... everything else above the fold is kind of just noise and it's super busy and I didn't know where to look."

---

### Don't Make Me Think

Apply Steve Krug's famous principle—your site should be self-explanatory without cognitive effort.

> "That first rule of like don't make me think I'm like having to think about like what are they trying to tell me."

> "Design rule number one of don't make me think... was it a classic book Steve Krug I think was the author."

**Bad Example:** [Artisan AI](#case-study-artisan-ai) — "Talked about digital workers I think Artisans and I'm like what's an artisan."

**Bad Example:** [Roll Stack](#case-study-roll-stack) — "These diagrams are technical there's icons you try to have to understand like what are the references of the icons."

---

### Use Simple Language, Avoid Jargon

Technical language and industry jargon alienate visitors who don't already understand your space.

> "Simple language communicate very clearly what is the problem that you're solving and how do you solve it what is the benefit for your users."

> "The phrases were like jargon that weren't familiar to me."

> "Now we're getting into like smaller text that's really light gray color on a gray text in a black background is hard to read... too much jargon."

**Bad Example:** [Cloud Thread](#case-study-cloud-thread) — "Activate finops bridge the gap between like these are all just kind of like very vague fluffy kind of things that don't give me clarity in what they actually do."

**Bad Example:** [Integrated Reasoning](#case-study-integrated-reasoning) — "One-of-a-kind High throughput processors designed for combinatorial optimization... I don't know what this company does."

---

### Explain Both the Problem AND the Solution

Don't just state the problem exists—clearly communicate how you fix it.

> "It's not telling us like what they do to fix it... I don't know the problem either though... we don't know exactly what we're fixing and we don't know what they do to fix it."

**Bad Example:** [Cloud Thread](#case-study-cloud-thread) — "The entire above the fold is explaining to us a... it's not telling us like what they do to fix it."

---

### Match Visuals to Your Message

Background images and visuals should reinforce, not contradict, your core message.

> "I'm a sucker for like hero backgrounds and that was like this beautiful nature scenery but nothing about the background or the headline said cleaning services."

**Bad Example:** [Ampstem](#case-study-ampstem) — "The outdoors are just I think like allergy medication I think like everything but cleaning service."

---

### Tell Visitors Who You're For

If your product is location-specific or for a particular audience, say so immediately.

> "They're in Nigeria... that might be good information to put at the top... because maybe somebody in the US or another country is interested in cleaning services and they're not actually a potential customer."

**Good Context:** [Integrated Reasoning](#case-study-integrated-reasoning) — "It's possible that the language that they're using there is actually the right language for their customer... and that the type of people that need this product would instantly understand that."

---

### Differentiate from Competitors

Saying you do something isn't enough when others do it too. Explain why you're different.

> "There's so many different cleaning services and cleaning companies like why should anybody use this one versus any others and just continuing to say we do cleaning services is not going to be the thing that's going to get somebody to choose them over."

**Bad Example:** [Ampstem](#case-study-ampstem) — Generic "cleaning services" messaging without differentiation.

## Visual Hierarchy & Layout

### Don't Let Color Dominate Over Message

When asked what they remembered, the first word from both reviewers was a color—not the product or value proposition. Strong colors can overpower your actual message.

> "Purple that's exactly the word I was going to say."

**Bad Example:** [Artisan AI](#case-study-artisan-ai) — The dominant purple color was more memorable than what the product actually does.

---

### Avoid the Paradox of Choice with CTAs

Multiple calls-to-action don't increase clicks—they paralyze visitors.

> "There's a bunch of different call to actions here too which I think sometimes when people are designing websites they're like let's put a bunch on here because certainly we'll get the user with one of them but instead what happens is you end up kind of creating the Paradox of choice a lot of times where people just don't click on any of them."

**Bad Example:** [Artisan AI](#case-study-artisan-ai) — Multiple competing CTAs above the fold.

---

### Use Space to Communicate, Not Decorate

Every element should serve a purpose. Don't waste valuable real estate on pure decoration.

> "Probably 80% of the page was just that like wavy background thing there was empty space that could have been used to like communicate more about what they do or get somebody excited to want to learn more."

> "There was like a preloader too and it waited for the preloader to load that wavy thing and so the wavy thing wasn't worth waiting for it to load."

**Bad Example:** [Integrated Reasoning](#case-study-integrated-reasoning) — Decorative wavy animation dominated the hero without conveying information.

---

### Make Text Readable

Small, low-contrast text strains readers and causes them to skip content entirely.

> "The text was really small too... I had to strain my eyes to get in there and read the the light gray very small text."

> "The sub headline and the text on the buttons could probably be larger... because they have the space and there's not a lot of words and wouldn't have to strain as much."

> "The white text on this color blue from my aesthetic was a little hard to read."

> "Old man eyes over here." (on struggling to read light gray text on black background)

**Bad Example:** [Integrated Reasoning](#case-study-integrated-reasoning) — Very small, light gray text on the hero.

**Bad Example:** [Roll Stack](#case-study-roll-stack) — White text on blue background with poor contrast.

**Bad Example:** [Cloud Thread](#case-study-cloud-thread) — "Smaller text that's really light gray color on a gray text in a black background is hard to read."

---

### Show the Product

If you have a physical product or clear UI, show it. Don't just describe it.

> "The illustration is a little bit just kind of plain kind of basic... it'd be cool to kind of see like what's the product right cuz I got the messaging... but I kind of want to see what the product looks like."

> "Is this a new heat pump I have to buy and replace my existing one is this an add-on... is it Hardware is there software involved here... that's not super clear to me."

**Needs Improvement:** [Capacity](#case-study-capacity) — Clear messaging but no product visualization.

**Good Example:** [Bottomless](#case-study-bottomless) — "I saw physical products there... it looked like a trustworthy brand."

---

### Screenshots vs. Stylized Illustrations: Choose Wisely

Deciding whether to show actual screenshots or stylized illustrations is tricky. Wireframe-style graphics can be hard to parse, especially with poor contrast.

> "It's always tricky to figure out like should you show screenshots... or as they're doing here you can see they kind of have these like pencil outlined like browser windows that they're trying to show."

> "Because it's this little white border on a the blue background it's harder to tell like what that actually is unless you really focus in on it."

**Bad Example:** [Roll Stack](#case-study-roll-stack) — Pencil-outlined browser windows on blue background were hard to distinguish.

---

### Put Your Best Content Above the Fold

Often the clearest explanation is buried below where visitors never see it.

> "Look at this if you scroll down like that should be the headline and it's below the fold... everything else above the fold is kind of just noise... this one's like super clean and really just kill all that and just make this the hero."

**Bad Example:** [Artisan AI](#case-study-artisan-ai) — The clearest messaging was hidden below the fold.

---

## Animation & Motion

### Motion Draws the Eye—Use It Strategically

Animation is a powerful tool to direct attention to what matters most.

> "We are in an animated world right and so the traditional web has always been static text and static images."

> "Leveraging animation and bringing motion in and and just even that rotating those words where it said dog food like that drew my eye in and I knew that's where I was supposed to look."

> "Movement draws your eye... if there's things that you really want to make sure that people pay attention to right out of the gate put some motion around it have it animate in do something and that will draw the viewer's eye."

**Good Example:** [Bottomless](#case-study-bottomless) — Rotating words drew attention to product categories.

---

### Animation Should Simplify, Not Complicate

When animations are too complex or technical, they become barriers instead of aids.

> "If you're going to use the illustrations in the animations just make it really simple so it's just a no-brainer and quickly just explains like what what does the product do."

> "These diagrams are technical there's icons you try to have to understand like what are the references of the icons... there's multiple browser windows... so it's a lot to digest it's kind of technical."

**Bad Example:** [Roll Stack](#case-study-roll-stack) — "There's a lot of detail they're trying to push into the illustrations that it's just it's just hard to digest."

---

### Don't Let Motion Distract from the Message

Animation that competes with your headline defeats its purpose.

> "The last one we talked about motion this one had motion but the motion distracted me from the main message."

> "I just spent so much time trying to read the sideways moving text that I didn't even read the like really big letters."

**Bad Example:** [Cloud Thread](#case-study-cloud-thread) — "I didn't really catch what it did."

**Bad Example:** [Integrated Reasoning](#case-study-integrated-reasoning) — "Really cool animations but the animations aren't really doing a job of telling the story."

---

### Keep Moving Text Short and Slow Enough to Read

Fast-moving sentences are impossible to process in real time.

> "The motion had too much text and it was moving too quickly and like I just couldn't process."

> "When something's moving you kind of want each phrase to be really short and easy to digest... if you have to read each of those sentences you're it's moving too fast so you kind of just skip over it."

**Bad Example:** [Cloud Thread](#case-study-cloud-thread) — "I didn't know I didn't know the words either like the phrases were like jargon that weren't familiar to me."

---

### Avoid Multiple Simultaneous Animations

When everything moves at once, nothing gets attention.

> "There's lots of animations going on at the same time and when there's lots of animations going on at the same time then it makes you go like what should I focus on right now and kind of pulls you all over the place."

> "There is a lot that's moving here... this is another interesting case where there's lots of animations going on at the same time."

**Bad Example:** [Roll Stack](#case-study-roll-stack) — "I spent a long time on the animation here which kind of took me away from some of the other stuff."

---

### Animations Should Guide Users, Not Just Look Cool

The best animations help illustrate concepts that are hard to explain with static content.

> "The best animations are the ones that guide you to a very specific thing... that like you want the user to take away or that helps to better illustrate a concept that is hard to do with static text or images."

> "Somebody really enjoyed making these but the animations really kind of distract from telling the story of of what this company does."

**Bad Example:** [Integrated Reasoning](#case-study-integrated-reasoning) — "Amazing animations... but I still don't really understand what they do even getting to the bottom of it."

---

### Watch Out for Looping Animations Mid-Story

If visitors arrive mid-loop, they lack context to understand what's happening.

> "There's a lot of stuff moving really fast and if I'm not watching it the entire way through then I feel like I've missed something and I'm starting halfway through and I don't have the context for like what's actually happening."

**Bad Example:** [Roll Stack](#case-study-roll-stack) — Complex looping animation confused new viewers.

---

## Technical Performance

### Page Speed Matters for SEO and First Impressions

Heavy images and slow loading hurt your Google ranking and lose visitors before they even see your content.

> "Big red flag there just for Bert Labs is that... Google's not going to favor that in search results just being a very heavy loading website."

> "The world has moved to mobile and so if you have a really heavy website on desktop on a fast broadband like we do here it's definitely going to be super slow on a cellular network and on a mobile phone."

> "Google has a series of criteria it looks at when it's ranking websites and page speed is one of those so you want your website to always load really really fast and snappy."

**Bad Example:** [Bert Labs](#case-study-bert-labs) — "The images were heavy... that's going to be um Google's not going to favor that."

---

### Good Domains are Memorable

A clear, memorable URL helps visitors return and builds brand trust.

> "Great domain right bottom.com oh yeah not even [a subdomain]."

> "I do like the domain though I think that... just The Branding is really important and having a URL that you can remember... I could probably remember that and come back and find."

**Good Example:** [Bottomless](#case-study-bottomless) — "bottom.com" domain.

**Good Example:** [Integrated Reasoning](#case-study-integrated-reasoning) — "reason.like reasoning" memorable URL.

# Case Studies

## Case Study: Artisan AI

**Website:** Artisan AI  
**Product:** Digital workers (AI assistants) for sales

### What Worked
- Video content likely contained valuable information (though not viewed in the test)

### What Failed
- **Color dominated first impression:** "Purple that's exactly the word I was going to say" — Both reviewers' first memory was the color, not the product
- **Two competing headlines:** "I didn't really know the hierarchy... I didn't know where to focus on two headlines"
- **Jargon-heavy language:** "Talked about digital workers I think Artisans and I'm like what's an artisan... that first rule of like don't make me think I'm like having to think about like what are they trying to tell me"
- **Best content below fold:** "If you scroll down like that should be the headline and it's below the fold... everything else above the fold is kind of just noise"
- **Two equal-weight columns:** "We have two equal weight columns and I think what that tells the user here is that both are equally important"
- **Multiple CTAs:** "There's a bunch of different call to actions here too... you end up kind of creating the Paradox of choice"
- **Unclear product category:** "I know it's an AI assistant but it didn't say that... and I wanted it to say that"
- **Busy visual design:** "It's super busy and I didn't know where to look"

### Key Lessons
- Don't let a single color dominate the first impression over your message
- Consolidate to one headline, one byline, one CTA
- Say what you are plainly (e.g., "AI sales assistant")
- Eliminate invented terminology that requires explanation
- Remove competing visual elements that split attention
- Move clearest messaging to above the fold

## Case Study: Bottomless

**Website:** Bottomless (bottom.com)  
**Product:** Smart scale that auto-reorders products when you run low

### What Worked
- **Clean design:** "It was clean"
- **Trustworthy brand appearance:** "It looked like a trustworthy brand"
- **Physical products visible:** "I saw physical products there"
- **Memorable rotating animation:** "That rotating those words where it said dog food like that drew my eye in and I knew that's where I was supposed to look"
- **Intriguing concept:** "Now that you're explaining it I want to click on how it works"
- **Great domain:** "Great domain right bottom.com"
- **Not confusing:** "It's not confusing"
- **Attention-grabbing motion:** "We remembered the different tea, kombucha, bananas, peanut butter and that's with us even after just few seconds"

### What Failed
- **Core differentiator not immediately clear:** "I missed the smart scale I didn't see the scale I thought it was... dog food"
- **Key value prop could be bigger:** "Repeat deliveries at the perfect time... that could be a little bigger"

### Key Lessons
- Strategic animation draws eyes to key information
- Clean design builds trust
- Physical products should be visible for hardware companies
- Could emphasize the "smart" differentiator more prominently
- Use motion to make product categories memorable

## Case Study: Capacity

**Website:** Capacity  
**Product:** Smart heat pump optimization

**Overall Assessment:** One of the strongest sites reviewed—clear, clean, and immediately understandable.

### What Worked
- **Clear, concise headline:** "There was one 2 3 four five words in the headline and like six words in the sub headline"
- **Immediately understandable:** "It seemed pretty straightforward... I think I know what this product does it seems like a smart heat pump"
- **Value prop clear:** "I think it's going to save me money on my electricity bill"
- **Clean design:** "It's pretty clean"
- **Relevant illustration:** "There's a little house thing over on the side"
- **Not overwhelming:** "They don't throw too much at us... not have a bunch of other information bombarding us and distracting us from the main things"
- **Easy to digest in 5 seconds:** "Within 5 seconds we easily read that and were able to digest the illustration"
- **Distilled copy:** "They definitely went through some Cycles with copyrighting here because this feels distilled and it definitely feels concise"

### What Failed
- **No product visualization:** "I kind of want to see what the product looks like... is this a new heat pump I have to buy and replace my existing one is this an add-on"
- **Text could be larger:** "The sub headline and the text on the buttons could probably be larger... because they have the space"
- **Basic illustration:** "The illustration is a little bit just kind of plain kind of basic"

### Key Lessons
- Few words, well chosen, beat lengthy explanations
- Match illustration complexity to message complexity
- Leave visitors wanting to learn more (a good problem)
- Show the actual product when possible
- Use available space to make text more readable

## Case Study: Roll Stack

**Website:** Roll Stack  
**Product:** Automated slide deck and document generation

### What Worked
- **Core concept came through:** "I think I know what it does... I think it's some kind of automation that pulls in multiple resources and automates some Pitch decks for you"
- **Communicates general purpose:** "Automate your slide decks and documents"

### What Failed
- **Too much simultaneous animation:** "There is a lot that's moving here... lots of animations going on at the same time... what should I focus on right now and kind of pulls you all over the place"
- **Animation distracted from content:** "I spent a long time on the animation here which kind of took me away from some of the other stuff"
- **Technical diagrams too complex:** "These diagrams are technical there's icons you try to have to understand like what are the references of the icons... there's multiple browser windows"
- **Wireframe illustrations hard to parse:** "They kind of have these like pencil outlined like browser windows that they're trying to show... because it's this little white border on a the blue background it's harder to tell like what that actually is unless you really focus in on it"
- **Too many small details:** "We got small icons there's just too much small stuff here... lot of details too where you're you need to try to digest like what each of these little pieces"
- **Unclear icon references:** "We have little icons at the bottom... I assume that's like different formats that they support but what does it represent"
- **Poor text contrast:** "The white text on this color blue from my aesthetic was a little hard to read"
- **Looping animation without context:** "If I'm not watching it the entire way through then I feel like I've missed something and I'm starting halfway through"
- **Floating percentages confuse:** "There's like a percentage that floats down from the roll stack... like what does that mean"
- **Implicit meaning requires guessing:** "What does that actually mean... if that's the point you want to make like just show really big icons and say we support these formats be very explicit"

### Key Lessons
- Limit simultaneous moving elements
- Simplify animated diagrams dramatically
- Avoid wireframe-style illustrations with poor contrast
- Make icons large and label them explicitly
- Don't make users decode meaning from complex visuals
- Ensure text contrast is strong against backgrounds
- Consider viewers who join animations mid-loop
- Remove ambiguous floating elements (like unexplained percentages)
