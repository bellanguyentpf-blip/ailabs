# Design Principles

## Messaging & Clarity

### Answer "What Is This?" First and Foremost

Your website must immediately communicate what your product actually does before anything else.

> "A lot of times for your website you want to be focused on communicating what is it first and foremost and then the user that hits the site they want to know is this for me."

> "We're both struggling here to figure out exactly what this is."

**Bad Example:** [Oda Studio](#case-study-oda-studio) — "Is this software? Do they send photographers to your place to take the photo? That's not immediately clear to me here."

---

### Pair "Is This For Me?" With "What Is It?"

Even if you nail audience targeting, visitors still need to understand your product.

> "I think they do a good job of the 'is it for me' because if I have a property that I need to take photos of I feel like oh this is interesting but it's not clear to me exactly what they do with those photos."

**Bad Example:** [Oda Studio](#case-study-oda-studio) — Good audience signal, but unclear product description.

---

### Avoid Overly Broad Taglines

Generic taglines fail to communicate specific value.

> "I think the tagline 'stand out with your property photos' kind of it's probably a little too broad."

**Bad Example:** [Oda Studio](#case-study-oda-studio) — "Stand out with your property photos" doesn't explain what the product does.

---

### Be Specific, Not Generic

Labels like "product example" waste opportunities to communicate value.

> "It's not clear. It's very generic just saying like 'product example.' They should say like 'here's how our software automatically adds furniture or enhances the quality of the photo or adds a crazy sky.'"

**Bad Example:** [Oda Studio](#case-study-oda-studio) — Used generic "product example" instead of explaining the value.

---

### Lead With Business Outcomes, Not Features

Connect your product to real business value rather than just describing what it does.

> "If you can communicate how your product is gonna help them get more rent or list it faster find a renter faster that's actually going to go a long way because instead of just talking about specific benefits you're actually talking about the value that you can provide which is like make more money get your listings rented faster."

**Good Example:** [Oda Studio (internal page)](#case-study-oda-studio) — Better approach would be: "Make more money, get your listings rented faster."

---

### Headlines Must Tell Your Story

Visitors scan headlines, so your headline sequence should communicate your complete value proposition.

> "I think if we were actually you know potential users of this product we would have gone I don't really know what's happening here and then we said okay... we would kind of keep skipping down and just reading the headlines and so it's really important that your website the headlines tell the story that you want to tell as you're scrolling down."

**Important:** [Fly Code](#case-study-fly-code) — Headlines should flow as a narrative when read in sequence.

---

### Unify Your Target Audience Messaging

Don't confuse visitors about whether the product is for them by targeting different personas in different sections.

> "One thing I noticed on the two almost competing headlines because the first very top one... is 'we free developers from product edits'... and then if you scroll down the second byline is actually 'we're for product teams'... maybe consolidating that value to you know product and Engineering teams."

> "That's the biggest thing people are going to hit this website and they're going to go 'is it for me' and I think... if I was just a product manager as my background the developer line might not resonate with me I might say like oh it's for developers it's not actually for me."

**Bad Example:** [Fly Code](#case-study-fly-code) — Mixed messaging between developers and product managers.

---

## Visual Hierarchy & Content Structure

### Put Your Best Content Higher on the Page

The most compelling explanations often get buried too far down.

> "This is where they should be on the homepage... yeah this is what we're looking for."

> "So website wise they're really missing out an opportunity to communicate some of this stuff more clearly."

> "Further down the page is the really valuable stuff and that should be pulled up a lot higher."

**Bad Example:** [Oda Studio](#case-study-oda-studio) — Best explanation buried on persona-specific pages, not the homepage.

---

### Order Social Proof After Explanation

Don't show social proof before visitors understand what you do.

> "Things like 'Oto Studio by the numbers' like I don't even have context to know what they do at this point and so that can go... that's probably a social proof attempt and social proof can go way down."

**Bad Example:** [Oda Studio](#case-study-oda-studio) — Stats appeared before product was explained.

---

### Users Are Comfortable Scrolling—Use Layers

Don't cram everything above the fold. Reveal information progressively.

> "I think actually a lot of users are comfortable scrolling and so I would think about it as unveiling different layers of the onion for what you want to communicate."

**Bad Example:** [Fly Code](#case-study-fly-code) — "It looks like what they're trying to do is cram all the key information up in the top as close to above the fold as they can."

---

### Avoid Overwhelming Text Blocks

Large text blocks will be skipped. Headlines get read; paragraphs often don't.

> "There's a lot of text here... yeah I don't think I would read this I would just look at I'd be like 'increase sales 64 make a human connection boost' and yeah it's kind of overwhelming with too much text."

**Bad Example:** [Colossian](#case-study-colossian) — Use case pages had too much dense text.

---

## Animation & Interaction

### Animations Draw the Eye—Use Them Intentionally

Motion attracts attention. Make sure it's highlighting what matters most.

> "The thing I noticed while the page loaded there was an animation so it kind of takes your your eyes there right away... maybe it distracts from what you really want them to read first."

> "Anytime you have animations and you have things that are moving on the page that always draws the viewer's eye and so it's a good idea to use that when you're trying to highlight something that you want to make sure that somebody pays attention to first but if you're doing it for something that's not the main thing you can be distracting from the main thing that you actually want somebody to focus on."

**Bad Example:** [Fly Code](#case-study-fly-code) — Animation on "three minutes" distracted from the main headline.

---

### Make Interactive Elements Obvious

If something is clickable or playable, make it visually clear.

> "This wasn't obvious to me that I could click on this to play it... I think like a big play button would be more obvious."

**Bad Example:** [Artifact](#case-study-artifact) — Audio sample didn't have a clear play button.

---

## Social Proof & Trust

### Real Faces, Titles, and Companies Build Trust

Social proof is most credible when it includes identifiable people.

> "Once I understand 'what is this, is it for me,' getting to some social proof around like does this work can I trust it... is really helpful."

> "I don't know maybe these are made up or whatever but like they put their face behind it they put their company name behind it so anytime I see a face and a title and a company it's... unless you're doing something really shady this is really seems like a real person."

**Good Example:** [Fly Code](#case-study-fly-code) — Included testimonials with photos, titles, and company names.

---

### Press Logos and Quotes Add Credibility

Third-party coverage significantly increases trust, especially for novel products.

> "Today Show CBS Wirecutter Wall Street Journal so like seems like they've gotten a lot of really impressive press which makes me take this seriously too."

> "A lot of the quotes are great because it says like 'memorable keepsakes shareable audio heirloom.'"

**Good Example:** [Artifact](#case-study-artifact) — Strong press logos and meaningful pull quotes.

---

### Show Review Counts and Ratings

Quantified social proof (reviews, ratings) adds credibility.

> "They've even got their reviews up here a thousand plus reviews nearly five stars there."

**Good Example:** [Artifact](#case-study-artifact) — Displayed 1000+ reviews with near-5-star rating.

---

## Product Demonstration

### Show How It Works Immediately—Seeing Is Believing

For novel products, a clear demonstration is worth more than any description.

> "The promise sounds really great... but the execution oftentimes is lacking and so the first thing that I want to do is see a video of this I just want to see if this actually works."

**Good Example:** [Colossian](#case-study-colossian) — "There's a video right here so let's check this out."

---

### Tell Users How Long the Demo Is

Disclosing video length reduces friction and increases willingness to watch.

> "I do actually like the fact this is one of the only websites I've seen where you know a lot of websites try to get to your watch videos where it's like 'it's only 15 seconds' and I think that puts your guard down... okay this is not going to take a lot of time."

**Good Example:** [Colossian](#case-study-colossian) — Showed 15-second video length upfront.

---

### Use Before/After Sliders Effectively

Before/after comparisons are powerful, but only if paired with explanation.

> "Here's an example... it's a good use of a slider."

> "This page goes a much longer way towards communicating what's actually... what the software is just by pairing the same exact slider you see a before and after photo that's the same photo and the value prop or what it actually is 'premium virtual staging.'"

**Good Example (with context):** [Oda Studio (persona page)](#case-study-oda-studio) — Slider paired with "premium virtual staging" explanation.

**Bad Example (without context):** [Oda Studio (homepage)](#case-study-oda-studio) — Slider without explanation of what changed.

---

### Be Precise About Demo Quality

Choose demo examples carefully—larger displays reveal flaws.

> "One thing that stands out to me with the demo is when it's bigger it looks less accurate like the mouth when it was smaller it was a little more incredible... so being really precise around what you're using as your showcase demo is really important."

**Bad Example:** [Colossian](#case-study-colossian) — Demo looked less convincing at larger size.

---

### Consider Letting Users Try Before Signing Up

Hands-on experience can be powerful for conversion.

> "I wonder if there's a way to just right here at the top have a text box that people can put their text into and just see it generated... and then you get your own content maybe you can't save it out yeah until you start paying that could be a great way to get people through the funnel."

**Opportunity:** [Colossian](#case-study-colossian) — Could offer free trial generation.

## Target Audience & Personas

### Persona Pages Need Clear Paths from Homepage

If you have persona-specific content, make sure visitors can easily find their path.

> "What page is this how did you get to it?... These go to different user personas that might use their software so I clicked on property manager."

**Structural Note:** [Oda Studio](#case-study-oda-studio) — Persona pages had better content than homepage.

---

### Consider Who Actually Buys

Understand where your messaging needs to resonate most.

> "Hello managers... so all of a sudden I kind of go from AI actors... and all of a sudden it's managers I'm guessing at companies to do like corporate training I wouldn't have expected this as being like the primary target."

**Observation:** [Colossian](#case-study-colossian) — Corporate training angle was unexpected but may be where the business is.

---

### Balance Developer and Non-Developer Messaging

When your product serves both technical and non-technical users, find unified value.

> "You want to speed up the designer developer workflows you don't want to like paint developers in negative light like 'avoid developers' or I know here it's sort of help developers work on other things so it's a... there's a lot of tension to navigate so you want to get the messaging right."

> "It's called fly code so it has something to do with code there's like benefits for non-developers developers freeing software Engineers there's some confusion here."

**Bad Example:** [Fly Code](#case-study-fly-code) — Name implied code, messaging implied no-code, creating confusion.

---

### Lead with Unique Differentiators

If you have a standout feature, don't bury it.

> "The multi-language portion really sells it for me potentially if you have a use case where you really want to create video content led by a host and you really needed that many languages it feels like this is the only way to really do that... and we had to go all the way down in order to find that and pull that out so that might be something to put up here too."

**Bad Example:** [Colossian](#case-study-colossian) — Multi-language capability was buried deep in the page.

# Case Studies

## Case Study: Oda Studio

**Website:** Property photo enhancement tool  
**Product:** Virtual staging and photo enhancement for property listings

### What Worked

- **Clean, simple design:** "One thing I do like about this site is just it's really straightforward in the design it's pretty simple it's... no frills no you know egregious animation."
- **Before/after slider UI:** "It's a good use of a slider."
- **Persona-specific pages:** The property manager page communicated value more effectively than the homepage.
- **Clear value prop (on internal pages):** "Premium virtual staging... I can immediately connect okay I can see how this is a solution for me."

### What Failed

- **Unclear product definition:** "Is this software? Do they send photographers to your place to take the photo? That's not immediately clear to me here."
- **Vague tagline:** "The tagline 'stand out with your property photos' kind of it's probably a little too broad."
- **Generic labels:** "It's very generic just saying like 'product example' they should say like 'here's how our software automatically adds furniture or enhances the quality of the photo.'"
- **Best content buried:** "This is where they should be on the homepage" (referring to persona page content).
- **Social proof too early:** "Things like 'Oto Studio by the numbers' like I don't even have context to know what they do at this point."
- **Missing outcome messaging:** "If you can communicate how your product is gonna help them get more rent or list it faster find a renter faster that's actually going to go a long way."

### Key Lessons

- Explain what your product actually does before anything else
- Replace generic labels with specific value propositions
- Pull the best content from internal pages up to the homepage
- Lead with business outcomes (more rent, faster listings), not just features
- Save social proof for after explanation

## Case Study: Fly Code

**Website:** No-code product editing tool  
**Product:** Allows product teams to make product changes without developer involvement

### What Worked

- **Clear core concept:** "I assume lets non-technical people... helps product teams work with software Engineers to ship their products faster."
- **Good social proof execution:** "We got some people here and some quotes... they put their face behind it they put their company name behind it... and we even got some it looks like some tweets."
- **Professional appearance:** "The website visually looks great like it looks professionally done and it sets a high bar for their product."

### What Failed

- **Competing audiences:** "Two almost competing headlines... 'we free developers from product edits'... then 'we're for product teams.'"
- **Unclear target user:** "I think if I was just a product manager as my background the developer line might not resonate with me I might say like oh it's for developers it's not actually for me."
- **Confusing animation:** "The thing I noticed while the page loaded there was an animation so it kind of takes your your eyes there right away... maybe it distracts from what you really want them to read first."
- **Cramming content above the fold:** "It looks like what they're trying to do is cram all the key information up in the top as close to above the fold as they can."
- **Name/product mismatch:** "It's called fly code so it has something to do with code there's like benefits for non-developers developers freeing software Engineers there's some confusion here."

### Key Lessons

- Unify messaging for a single primary audience, then expand
- Be careful with motion—it draws attention, so use it on the most important element
- Users scroll; use that to layer information progressively
- Consider if your company name aligns with your value proposition
- "When you're designing your page the headlines are the things that are really going to stand out"

## Case Study: Colossian

**Website:** AI video generation platform  
**Product:** Creates videos with AI-generated actors and voices

### What Worked

- **Clear hero messaging:** "Create videos with AI actors... pretty clear what they do."
- **Demo video with length shown:** "I do actually like the fact this is one of the only websites I've seen where... 'it's only 15 seconds' and I think that puts your guard down."
- **Using own product to demonstrate:** "It looks like they're using their own product to to demo the videos to sell their own product."
- **Strong unique differentiator:** "The multi-language portion really sells it for me."
- **Cool tech appeal:** "We look at this and we're like wow this is really cool Tech."

### What Failed

- **Uncanny valley effect:** "It's got that uncanny valley... effect."
- **Unexpected persona targeting:** "Hello managers... I'm guessing at companies to to do like corporate training I wouldn't have expected this as being like the primary target."
- **Unfinished use case pages:** "It looks like the use cases don't directly match... this is a little maybe feels a little unfinished."
- **Too much text:** "There's a lot of text here... yeah I don't think I would read this."
- **Demo quality degraded at size:** "When it's bigger it looks less accurate like the mouth... when it was smaller it was a little more incredible."
- **Key differentiator buried:** "We had to go all the way down in order to find that and pull that out so that might be something to put up here too."

### Key Lessons

- Show video length upfront to increase willingness to watch
- Use your own product to demonstrate value
- Be careful with demo sizing—test at all sizes
- Pull unique differentiators (like multi-language support) higher on the page
- Keep text blocks scannable; use headlines people will actually read
- Consider offering try-before-you-buy experiences

## Case Study: Artifact

**Website:** Family audio keepsake service  
**Product:** Professional interviewers create podcast-style recordings about your children

**Overall Assessment:** "Very cool... very awesome seems like something I'd probably give it a try."

### What Worked

- **Emotional hook:** "Beautiful... very grabs you on an emotional level."
- **Clear value prop:** "She only grows up once I don't want to forget it artifact creates a personal podcast series about your child... cool I get what it is."
- **Impressive press coverage:** "Today Show CBS Wirecutter Wall Street Journal so like seems like they've gotten a lot of really impressive press which makes me take this seriously."
- **Quality sample content:** "I like the background music it sounds like good quality... that seems kind of professional."
- **Strong review count:** "They've even got their reviews up here a thousand plus reviews nearly five stars."
- **Audience targeting:** "If you don't have a child it's not for you if you have a child it's for you."
- **Gift positioning:** "Memorable keepsakes shareable audio heirloom... beautiful gift."
- **Visual quality:** "It's very beautiful it's very... looks great quality."

### What Failed

- **Initial product confusion:** "It's not immediately clear to me why I would want a personal podcast series about my child."
- **Hidden play button:** "This wasn't obvious to me that I could click on this to play it... I think like a big play button would be more obvious."
- **Unclear delivery mechanism:** "How do I... do you have to play it through their app?... I'm guessing you send this out to family or just keep it to archival."
- **Service nature not immediately clear:** "This was a service... we interview you about your child a pro-interviewer... this is a professionally produced interview with a parent about their child... that is not what I expected from the personalized podcast."

### Key Lessons

- Emotional hooks work, but still explain the product clearly
- Make audio/video play buttons obviously clickable
- Clarify the delivery mechanism (app, web, etc.)
- When your product model is unique (professional interviews vs. user-recorded), explain it earlier
- Strong press logos and reviews build significant trust
- Visual quality and polish communicate product quality
